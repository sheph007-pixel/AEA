# Compliance Alert: ACA Reporting Deadlines and Requirements

Applicable Large Employers (ALEs) are required to report information about the health coverage they offered to full-time employees during the prior calendar year. This alert summarizes the deadlines, filing requirements, common pitfalls, and penalty exposure associated with 2024 tax year ACA reporting.

## Who Must File

An employer is an ALE for a given calendar year if it employed an average of 50 or more full-time employees (including full-time equivalent employees) during the prior calendar year. All members of a controlled group or affiliated service group are aggregated for this purpose, even if individual entities within the group have fewer than 50 employees.

ALEs must file Forms 1094-C (the transmittal form) and 1095-C (the employee statement) with the IRS, and must furnish a copy of Form 1095-C to each individual who was a full-time employee for any month during the reporting year.

## Key Deadlines for 2024 Tax Year Reporting

- **Employee furnishing deadline:** The statutory deadline for furnishing Form 1095-C to full-time employees is March 3, 2025 (because the standard March 1 deadline falls on a Saturday). Employers should monitor IRS announcements for any extensions, but should plan to meet the statutory deadline.
- **IRS electronic filing deadline:** March 31, 2025, for employers filing electronically. Employers filing 10 or more information returns of any type during the calendar year are generally required to file electronically under the revised threshold established by the IRS.
- **IRS paper filing deadline:** February 28, 2025, for employers eligible to file on paper (those filing fewer than 10 information returns). In practice, most ALEs will exceed this threshold and must file electronically.

## Electronic Filing Requirements

The IRS has significantly lowered the threshold for mandatory electronic filing of information returns. As of tax year 2024, employers that file 10 or more information returns (including W-2s, 1099s, and 1095-Cs, among others) in aggregate during the calendar year must file electronically. This means that virtually all ALEs are now required to e-file their ACA returns.

Employers filing electronically submit through the IRS Affordable Care Act Information Returns (AIR) system. Many employers use payroll vendors, benefits administrators, or third-party ACA reporting providers to handle the electronic filing process. Employers should confirm that their filing agent has the necessary IRS transmitter control code and has tested successfully with the AIR system.

## Common Errors to Avoid

ACA reporting errors can result in rejected filings, IRS penalty notices, or both. Common issues include:

**Incorrect Line 14 coding.** Line 14 of Form 1095-C reports the code for the offer of coverage made to the employee for each month. Errors frequently arise when employers use the wrong code for the type of coverage offered, fail to update codes when an employee's status changes mid-year, or use code 1H (no offer of coverage) when coverage was in fact offered.

**Affordability calculation mistakes.** When using one of the affordability safe harbors (W-2, rate of pay, or federal poverty line), employers must apply the safe harbor consistently and correctly. Using the wrong employee cost figure or applying the wrong poverty line amount can result in inaccurate reporting.

**Missing or incorrect Social Security numbers.** The IRS matches 1095-C forms to individual tax returns using Social Security numbers. Missing, truncated, or incorrect SSNs can cause processing errors. Employers should verify SSNs against W-2 records and request corrections from employees where discrepancies exist.

**Failure to report all full-time employees.** Every individual who was a full-time employee for any month during the calendar year must receive a 1095-C, even if they declined coverage, terminated employment mid-year, or were in a waiting period. Employers should use a consistent methodology for identifying full-time employees under the ACA's monthly measurement method or look-back measurement method.

**Controlled group reporting errors.** Members of a controlled group must coordinate their reporting. The parent entity or designated government entity files the authoritative transmittal (Form 1094-C), and each member entity files its own 1094-C and 1095-Cs for its employees. Errors in identifying the correct controlled group structure can cascade through the entire filing.

## Penalty Exposure

The IRS may impose penalties under Internal Revenue Code Sections 6721 and 6722 for failures to file correct information returns with the IRS and failures to furnish correct payee statements, respectively. For returns required to be filed in 2025 (for the 2024 tax year), the penalty amounts are adjusted annually for inflation and can be substantial.

Penalties may apply for late filing, failure to file, and filing with incorrect information. Separate penalties may apply for the IRS filing and the employee furnishing obligations. Penalty amounts are higher for intentional disregard of the filing requirements.

In addition to information return penalties, employers that fail to offer affordable minimum essential coverage to substantially all full-time employees may be subject to employer shared responsibility payments under Section 4980H. Accurate ACA reporting is the primary mechanism through which the IRS assesses these potential payments.

## Recommended Steps

- Verify that all full-time employees for 2024 have been identified and included in the filing.
- Review 1095-C coding for accuracy, paying particular attention to Line 14 and Line 16 codes.
- Confirm affordability calculations using the chosen safe harbor method.
- Validate employee Social Security numbers against existing records.
- Confirm electronic filing capability and test submissions through the IRS AIR system or through your filing vendor.
- Retain copies of all filed forms and furnishing records for at least three years.
- Consult counsel or a tax advisor if you receive a Letter 226-J or other IRS correspondence related to ACA reporting.
