# Monthly Employer Briefing: February 2025

February is a critical compliance month for employers. Several reporting and posting deadlines converge, and ongoing developments in state paid leave laws and non-compete restrictions continue to reshape the employment landscape. This briefing highlights the key issues employers should have on their radar.

## ACA 1095-C Distribution Deadlines

Applicable Large Employers (ALEs) are required to furnish Form 1095-C to all individuals who were full-time employees for any month during the preceding calendar year. The statutory deadline for furnishing 2024 forms to employees is March 3, 2025 (the first business day after March 1, which falls on a Saturday).

In prior years, the IRS extended this deadline, sometimes through year-end legislation and sometimes through administrative relief. Employers should not assume that an extension will be granted and should prepare to meet the statutory deadline unless the IRS officially announces otherwise.

The electronic filing deadline for Forms 1094-C and 1095-C with the IRS is March 31, 2025 for electronic filers. Employers filing 10 or more information returns during the calendar year are generally required to file electronically.

Common issues that arise during ACA reporting include:

- Incorrect coding of employee offer of coverage (Line 14 of Form 1095-C)
- Failure to account for employees who transition between full-time and part-time status mid-year
- Errors in the affordability safe harbor calculations
- Missing or incorrect employee Social Security numbers

Employers should build in time for data review and correction before the filing deadline.

## OSHA 300A Posting Requirements

Employers who are required to maintain OSHA injury and illness records must post the OSHA Form 300A, the Summary of Work-Related Injuries and Illnesses, in a conspicuous location in the workplace from February 1 through April 30. The summary must cover the prior calendar year (2024) and must be signed by a company executive.

The posting requirement applies to employers with 11 or more employees at any time during the prior calendar year, with certain industry exemptions. Employers in partially exempt low-hazard industries should check the current OSHA exemption list to determine whether they are required to maintain records.

Additionally, employers who are required to electronically submit injury and illness data to OSHA should note that the submission deadline is typically March 2. The scope of electronic submission requirements has expanded in recent years, and employers should confirm whether they are covered.

## State Paid Leave Expansions

The trend toward state-mandated paid leave continues to accelerate. As of early 2025, a growing number of states have enacted paid family and medical leave (PFML) programs, paid sick leave laws, or both. Recent developments include:

**New programs taking effect.** Several states have been phasing in paid family and medical leave programs that involve payroll contributions leading up to the availability of benefits. Employers in states with newer programs should confirm whether they are in a contribution phase, a benefits phase, or both, and ensure that payroll deductions and employer contributions are being handled correctly.

**Expanded coverage.** Some states with existing paid sick leave laws have expanded their scope---for example, by lowering the employee-count threshold for coverage or broadening the definition of covered family members.

**Interaction with existing policies.** Employers who offer their own paid time off, sick leave, or short-term disability benefits should carefully analyze how state-mandated programs interact with their existing plans. In some states, private plans may be approved as substitutes for the state program if they meet certain equivalency requirements. In others, state benefits run concurrently or supplement employer-provided leave.

Employers operating in multiple states should maintain a jurisdiction-by-jurisdiction summary of applicable leave requirements and review it regularly.

## Non-Compete Agreement Landscape

The legal landscape for non-compete agreements remains in transition. In 2024, the Federal Trade Commission (FTC) issued a final rule that would have banned most non-compete agreements nationwide. However, federal courts blocked the rule from taking effect, and its future remains uncertain.

Regardless of the status of federal action, many states have independently imposed restrictions on non-compete agreements:

- **California, Minnesota, North Dakota, and Oklahoma** generally prohibit non-compete agreements for employees.
- **States including Colorado, Illinois, Maine, Maryland, New Hampshire, Oregon, Virginia, and Washington** have enacted laws restricting non-competes based on factors such as employee income level, notice requirements, or consideration requirements.
- Several additional states introduced non-compete legislation in their 2024 or 2025 legislative sessions.

Employers who use non-compete agreements should review them for compliance with applicable state laws. Agreements that were enforceable when signed may no longer be enforceable if the law has changed. Employers should also consider whether other restrictive covenants---such as non-solicitation agreements, confidentiality agreements, or garden leave provisions---may serve their legitimate business interests with less legal risk.

## Looking Ahead

As the first quarter progresses, employers should keep the following items on their radar:

- **EEO-1 reporting.** The EEOC typically opens the EEO-1 Component 1 data collection in the spring. Covered employers should begin preparing workforce demographic data.
- **State legislative sessions.** Many state legislatures are in session, and employment-related bills are being introduced at a rapid pace. Topics to watch include pay transparency, AI regulation, independent contractor classification, and workplace safety.
- **Benefits plan review.** Mid-Q1 is a good time to review benefit plan operations, confirm that required notices have been distributed, and assess whether plan amendments are needed.

## Key Action Items

- **Prepare and distribute Form 1095-C** to full-time employees by the March 3 statutory deadline.
- **Post OSHA Form 300A** in the workplace from February 1 through April 30.
- **Verify OSHA electronic submission** requirements and prepare data for the March 2 deadline.
- **Review paid leave obligations** in all states where employees work, and update policies as needed.
- **Audit non-compete agreements** for compliance with current state law.
- **Begin EEO-1 data preparation** for the anticipated spring filing window.
