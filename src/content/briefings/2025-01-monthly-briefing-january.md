# Monthly Employer Briefing: January 2025

The start of a new year brings a wave of regulatory changes and compliance deadlines that employers should be prepared to address. This briefing covers the most significant developments taking effect in January 2025 and outlines priorities for the first quarter.

## State Minimum Wage Changes Effective January 1

Multiple states and localities adjusted their minimum wage rates effective January 1, 2025. States including New York, California, Washington, and others implemented scheduled increases, many tied to inflation indexing formulas established by prior legislation. Employers operating in more than one jurisdiction should verify that payroll systems reflect the correct rates for each location where employees work.

Beyond base rate changes, employers should review whether tipped minimum wage rates, youth or training wages, or local ordinances impose additional requirements. Some cities and counties maintain minimum wages that exceed state levels, and employers are generally required to comply with whichever rate is highest.

Practical steps include updating payroll configurations, revising offer letters and job postings, ensuring wage notices are distributed where required by state law, and replacing workplace postings with current versions. Many states require employers to display updated minimum wage posters, and failure to post current notices may result in penalties.

## Updated OSHA Electronic Recordkeeping Requirements

The Occupational Safety and Health Administration (OSHA) has continued to expand its electronic recordkeeping and submission requirements. Employers in certain industries with 100 or more employees may be required to electronically submit information from their OSHA Form 300 (Log of Work-Related Injuries and Illnesses) and Form 301 (Injury and Illness Incident Report) in addition to the Form 300A summary that was previously required.

Employers who are covered by OSHA recordkeeping requirements should confirm whether they fall within the scope of the electronic submission mandate. The submission is generally due by March 2 of the year following the calendar year covered by the forms. Employers should use this time to review their injury and illness logs for accuracy, ensure that all recordable incidents have been properly documented, and correct any errors before submission.

It is also worth noting that OSHA has indicated it intends to publish certain establishment-specific data collected through electronic submissions. Employers may wish to consult counsel about the implications of this disclosure policy.

## ACA Reporting Season Preparation

Applicable Large Employers (ALEs)---generally those with 50 or more full-time or full-time equivalent employees---are required to file Forms 1094-C and 1095-C with the IRS and furnish copies to employees. The employee furnishing deadline for the 2024 tax year has historically been extended in prior years, but employers should plan against the statutory deadline unless and until the IRS announces relief.

Employers should begin gathering the data needed for accurate reporting now. This includes reviewing monthly employee counts, tracking offers of coverage, and confirming affordability calculations. Common errors in ACA reporting include incorrect coding of employee status, failure to account for mid-year changes in coverage, and inaccurate entry of employer identification numbers. Given that IRS penalty assessments under Sections 6721 and 6722 can be significant, careful preparation is advisable.

Employers who have not yet selected a reporting method---whether in-house, through payroll providers, or through third-party administrators---should finalize those arrangements early in the quarter.

## Employer Planning Priorities for Q1

The first quarter is an important window for employers to set the tone for the year. Several planning considerations deserve attention:

**Handbook and Policy Reviews.** Employment laws change frequently at both the state and federal level. Employers should review employee handbooks to ensure policies on leave, anti-harassment, remote work, social media, and other topics remain current. States including Illinois, California, and Minnesota enacted various employment law changes in 2024 that may require handbook updates.

**Benefit Plan Compliance.** Employers sponsoring group health plans should confirm that plan documents, summary plan descriptions, and required notices are up to date. Compliance with the Mental Health Parity and Addiction Equity Act (MHPAEA), transparency requirements, and prescription drug reporting obligations continues to evolve.

**I-9 and Work Authorization.** Employers should audit I-9 practices and ensure that any temporary COVID-era flexibilities that have expired are no longer being relied upon. Current I-9 form versions should be used for all new hires and reverifications.

**Performance Review Cycles.** For employers that conduct annual performance reviews, Q1 is a common time to finalize evaluations from the prior year and set goals for the current one. Consistent and well-documented performance management can be an important factor in defending against employment claims.

## Key Action Items

- **Verify minimum wage rates** in every state and locality where employees work; update payroll, postings, and wage notices accordingly.
- **Review OSHA logs** for completeness and accuracy; confirm whether electronic submission is required and note the March 2 deadline.
- **Begin ACA reporting preparation** by gathering coverage and workforce data for the 2024 tax year.
- **Audit employee handbooks** for compliance with new state and federal employment law developments.
- **Confirm I-9 practices** are consistent with current USCIS requirements and form versions.
- **Schedule Q1 compliance training** for managers and HR staff on topics such as anti-harassment, wage and hour, and workplace safety.
- **Review benefit plan documents** and required disclosures for ongoing compliance.
