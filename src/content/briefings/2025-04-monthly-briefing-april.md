# Monthly Employer Briefing: April 2025

April marks the start of the second quarter and an opportunity for employers to take stock of compliance obligations, review workplace safety programs, and prepare for the regulatory activity that typically intensifies in the spring. This briefing addresses five priority areas for employers this month.

## Spring Compliance Review

The transition from Q1 to Q2 is a practical checkpoint for employers to verify that first-quarter obligations have been met and to prepare for upcoming deadlines:

**OSHA 300A posting.** The OSHA Form 300A must remain posted in the workplace through April 30. Employers should verify that the summary is still displayed and has not been inadvertently removed.

**ACA filing confirmation.** Employers who filed Forms 1094-C and 1095-C electronically by the March 31 deadline should monitor for IRS acceptance or rejection notices through the AIR system. Rejected filings must be corrected and resubmitted promptly.

**EEO-1 filing.** The EEOC typically opens the EEO-1 data collection in the spring. Covered employers should watch for the opening announcement and be prepared to submit their workforce demographic data within the filing window.

**Quarterly tax filings.** Employers should ensure that Form 941 (Employer's Quarterly Federal Tax Return) is filed by April 30 for the first quarter of 2025. State quarterly wage and tax filings are also typically due around this time.

**Handbook acknowledgments.** If the employer updated its handbook or policies in Q1, verifying that employees have received and acknowledged the updated materials is a worthwhile administrative step.

## Workplace Safety Awareness

April brings National Safety Stand-Down to Prevent Falls in Construction (typically held in May but often preceded by employer preparation activities in April), and serves as a general reminder for all employers to refresh their safety practices.

**Heat illness prevention.** As temperatures begin to rise, employers with outdoor workers or employees in hot indoor environments should review their heat illness prevention plans. Several states, including California, Washington, and Oregon, have specific heat illness prevention standards. At the federal level, OSHA has been pursuing a proposed heat standard and continues to enforce heat-related hazards under the General Duty Clause. Employers should ensure that workers have access to water, shade, rest, and training on recognizing heat-related illness.

**Ergonomic assessments.** For office and administrative workers---particularly those in remote or hybrid arrangements---spring is a good time to check in on workstation ergonomics. Employers may consider offering ergonomic assessments or self-assessment checklists to remote employees.

**Emergency preparedness.** Spring weather can bring tornadoes, severe storms, and flooding in many regions. Employers should review emergency action plans, verify that evacuation routes are clear and marked, and conduct drills where appropriate. Plans should account for all shifts and work locations, including remote workers.

**Safety committee meetings.** Employers with safety committees should ensure that meetings are held regularly and documented. Some states require safety committees for employers above certain size thresholds, and maintaining consistent meeting schedules demonstrates a commitment to workplace safety.

## Immigration Enforcement Trends

Immigration enforcement and worksite investigations have been an area of increased focus. Employers should be aware of several developments:

**I-9 audits.** U.S. Immigration and Customs Enforcement (ICE) conducts Form I-9 inspections, sometimes referred to as "silent raids," in which employers receive a Notice of Inspection requiring them to produce I-9 forms for review within three business days. Employers should ensure that I-9 records are complete, accurate, and accessible. Common deficiencies found in audits include missing forms, late completion, failure to reverify expiring work authorization, and use of outdated form versions.

**E-Verify.** While E-Verify is not required for all employers at the federal level, a growing number of states require E-Verify for certain or all employers. Employers should determine whether they are subject to a mandatory E-Verify requirement based on state law, federal contractor status, or other factors.

**Anti-discrimination obligations.** While employers must verify work authorization, they must also avoid discrimination in the I-9 process. The Immigration and Nationality Act prohibits employers from requesting specific documents, rejecting valid documents, or treating employees differently in the verification process based on national origin or citizenship status. The Department of Justice's Immigrant and Employee Rights Section (IER) investigates and prosecutes document abuse and unfair documentary practices.

**Staying current.** Employers should monitor developments in immigration policy, as enforcement priorities, form requirements, and program rules may shift. Maintaining a consistent, lawful I-9 process---applied equally to all new hires---is the most reliable compliance approach.

## Mid-Year Benefits Review

April is an appropriate time for employers to conduct a mid-year review of their benefits programs:

**Plan performance.** Review health plan utilization data, claims experience, and costs against projections. Identify any trends that may affect renewal pricing or plan design decisions later in the year.

**Compliance obligations.** Verify that required notices have been distributed, including Summary of Benefits and Coverage (SBC) documents, HIPAA privacy notices, Women's Health and Cancer Rights Act notices, and any state-specific required disclosures. Confirm that the plan is operating in compliance with the Mental Health Parity and Addiction Equity Act (MHPAEA) and applicable transparency requirements.

**Prescription Drug Reporting.** The Consolidated Appropriations Act requires group health plans and insurers to submit certain prescription drug data to the federal government. Employers should confirm whether their plan has met the most recent reporting deadline and is prepared for future reporting cycles.

**FSA and HSA administration.** Check that flexible spending account and health savings account contributions are being processed correctly and that employees are aware of plan deadlines, carryover provisions, and rollover rules.

**Life events and enrollment.** Ensure that qualifying life events (such as marriage, birth, or loss of other coverage) are being processed promptly and that employees are informed of their rights to make mid-year enrollment changes.

## State Legislative Session Activity

As of April 2025, many state legislatures are at or approaching the midpoint of their sessions. Employment law bills are advancing on a wide range of topics:

**Pay transparency.** Additional states are considering legislation that would require employers to disclose pay ranges in job postings or upon employee request. Employers with multi-state operations should track these developments to prepare for potential new requirements.

**Paid leave expansion.** New paid family and medical leave programs and expanded paid sick leave laws continue to be introduced in multiple states. Employers should watch for developments in states where they have employees and plan for potential payroll system changes.

**AI in employment.** Legislation addressing the use of artificial intelligence in hiring and employment decisions is being considered in several states, following the models established by New York City and Colorado. Employers who use or are considering AI-powered HR tools should monitor these proposals closely.

**Cannabis and employment.** As more states legalize recreational cannabis, associated employment law questions---including drug testing policies, reasonable accommodation, and impairment standards---continue to evolve. Employers should review their substance abuse policies in light of current state law.

**Workplace violence prevention.** Additional states are considering workplace violence prevention legislation, including requirements for written prevention plans, training, and incident reporting.

Employers should work with legal counsel or policy monitoring services to track legislation relevant to their operations and begin planning for compliance with laws likely to be enacted.

## Key Action Items

- **Verify OSHA 300A** remains posted through April 30 and confirm electronic submission was completed.
- **Monitor ACA filing status** and respond promptly to any IRS rejection notices.
- **Prepare for EEO-1 filing** when the EEOC opens the data collection window.
- **File Form 941** and state quarterly returns by April 30.
- **Review heat illness prevention** plans and safety training for the warm-weather season.
- **Audit I-9 records** for completeness and accuracy; confirm E-Verify obligations.
- **Conduct a mid-year benefits compliance check** and review plan performance data.
- **Track state legislative activity** for employment law developments that may require policy changes.
