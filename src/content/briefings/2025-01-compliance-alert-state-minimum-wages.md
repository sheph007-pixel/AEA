# Compliance Alert: State Minimum Wage Changes Effective January 2025

Effective January 1, 2025, numerous states and localities implemented minimum wage increases. Employers should verify that their pay practices, postings, and related documentation reflect current requirements in every jurisdiction where they have employees.

## States with January 2025 Increases

More than 20 states adjusted their minimum wage rates at the start of 2025. Many of these increases result from annual inflation-indexed adjustments or scheduled step increases enacted by prior legislation. Notable changes include:

- **California** increased its state minimum wage to $16.50 per hour.
- **New York** implemented increases that vary by region, with New York City, Long Island, and Westchester County rates differing from the rest of the state.
- **Washington** raised its minimum wage to $16.66 per hour, maintaining one of the highest state rates in the country.
- **Connecticut, New Jersey, and Delaware** each implemented scheduled increases under existing state law.
- **Arizona, Colorado, Maine, Missouri, Montana, Ohio, and several other states** with inflation-indexed minimum wages saw adjustments based on Consumer Price Index changes.

Employers should consult their state labor department websites or qualified counsel to confirm the exact rates applicable to their workforce, as rates may differ for tipped employees, minors, or workers in certain industries.

## Local Ordinances

In addition to state-level changes, dozens of cities and counties maintain their own minimum wage rates that exceed state minimums. Jurisdictions such as Seattle, Denver, Minneapolis, and several California cities have local ordinances that employers must follow. Where a local rate is higher than the state rate, the employer must generally pay the higher amount.

Employers with employees in multiple localities should map each work location to its applicable minimum wage and ensure payroll is configured correctly for each.

## What Employers Should Update

**Payroll Systems.** The most immediate step is to confirm that payroll systems and timekeeping configurations have been updated to reflect new rates. This applies to hourly wages, overtime calculations, and any pay codes tied to the minimum wage.

**Wage Notices.** Several states require employers to provide written wage notices to employees at the time of hire and when pay rates change. States including California, New York, and Minnesota have specific wage notice requirements. Employers should issue updated notices where applicable.

**Workplace Postings.** Federal and state law generally require employers to display current minimum wage posters in the workplace. Updated posters should be obtained from the applicable state labor agency and posted in conspicuous locations accessible to employees. For remote workers, some states permit electronic distribution of required postings.

**Offer Letters and Job Postings.** Employers should review open job postings and template offer letters to confirm that stated pay rates meet or exceed new minimums. With the growing number of states and localities requiring pay transparency in job postings, ensuring accuracy is particularly important.

## Multi-State Considerations

Employers operating across state lines face the challenge of tracking different rates, tip credits, youth minimums, and notice requirements in each jurisdiction. A systematic approach---such as maintaining a jurisdiction-by-jurisdiction compliance matrix---can help reduce the risk of errors.

Employers should also consider whether minimum wage increases trigger changes to other compensation obligations. For example, some state salary thresholds for overtime exemptions are tied to the minimum wage, meaning a minimum wage increase could affect exempt status determinations.

## Consequences of Non-Compliance

Failing to pay the correct minimum wage can expose employers to back pay liability, liquidated damages, civil penalties, and in some cases private lawsuits. Several states allow employees to recover double or treble damages for minimum wage violations, and class or collective actions are not uncommon in this area.

Employers who discover that employees were underpaid due to an outdated rate should correct the issue promptly and consider consulting counsel about the best approach for making employees whole.

## Recommended Steps

- Confirm the current minimum wage in every jurisdiction where employees work.
- Update payroll systems, wage notices, and workplace postings.
- Review tip credit practices and youth wage rates where applicable.
- Audit job postings and offer letter templates for pay rate accuracy.
- Consult counsel if there is any uncertainty about which rates apply to specific employees or locations.
