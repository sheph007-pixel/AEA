# Monthly Employer Briefing: March 2025

As the first quarter draws to a close, employers face a busy period of compliance activity, hiring, and planning for the months ahead. This briefing covers spring hiring considerations, EEO-1 reporting preparation, the state legislative landscape, workplace safety reviews, and priorities for Q2.

## Spring Hiring Trends

Historically, spring is one of the more active hiring periods for many employers. Industries such as hospitality, construction, landscaping, retail, and food service often ramp up seasonal hiring, while professional services and healthcare may fill positions that were budgeted at the start of the year.

Employers engaged in spring hiring should keep several compliance considerations in mind:

**Pay transparency requirements.** A growing number of states and localities now require employers to include pay ranges in job postings. As of early 2025, jurisdictions with pay transparency requirements include California, Colorado, Connecticut, Maryland, Nevada, New York (including New York City), Rhode Island, Washington, and several others. Employers posting positions in these areas should ensure their listings include the required compensation information.

**Ban-the-box and fair chance hiring laws.** Many jurisdictions restrict when and how employers may inquire about an applicant's criminal history. Requirements vary widely, from prohibiting criminal history questions on the initial application to limiting consideration of certain types of convictions. Employers should review the rules in each jurisdiction where they recruit and hire.

**I-9 completion.** Employers must complete Section 2 of Form I-9 within three business days of an employee's first day of work for pay. For seasonal and high-volume hiring, having a reliable I-9 process in place---including designated individuals trained to complete the form---is essential to avoid errors.

**Youth employment restrictions.** Employers hiring minors should be aware of both federal and state restrictions on hours of work, types of work, and required work permits or age certificates. State laws often impose stricter requirements than federal law, and violations can result in significant penalties.

## EEO-1 Reporting Preparation

The Equal Employment Opportunity Commission (EEOC) typically opens the EEO-1 Component 1 data collection in the spring, with a filing deadline that has varied in recent years. Private employers with 100 or more employees, and federal contractors with 50 or more employees and contracts of $50,000 or more, are generally required to file.

The EEO-1 report requires employers to categorize employees by job category, race, ethnicity, and sex. Employers should begin preparing this data in advance of the filing window to ensure accuracy. Key preparation steps include:

- Confirming which entities within the organization are required to file
- Reviewing job category assignments to ensure employees are classified in the correct EEO-1 categories
- Verifying that self-identification data is current and has been collected through voluntary, confidential processes
- Identifying the appropriate workforce snapshot period (typically a single payroll period)

Employers who have not filed in prior years should determine whether they meet the coverage thresholds and register in the EEOC's online filing system.

## State-Level Legislative Activity

Many state legislatures are in full session during the spring months, and employment-related legislation is moving in a number of jurisdictions. Topics that have been generating legislative activity in 2025 include:

**Paid leave.** Several states are considering new paid family and medical leave programs or expansions of existing paid sick leave laws.

**Artificial intelligence in employment.** Following the lead of jurisdictions like New York City and Colorado, additional states are considering legislation that would regulate the use of AI tools in hiring and other employment decisions.

**Non-compete restrictions.** The trend toward limiting non-compete agreements continues at the state level, with new proposals to ban or restrict their use.

**Worker classification.** Some states continue to consider legislation addressing the classification of independent contractors, including adoption of ABC tests or other frameworks.

**Workplace safety.** State-level workplace violence prevention laws, heat illness prevention standards, and other safety-related proposals remain active in several legislatures.

Employers should monitor legislative developments in the states where they operate, as new laws may require policy changes, training, or system updates.

## Workplace Safety Program Reviews

March is a practical time for employers to conduct a comprehensive review of their workplace safety programs before the busy spring and summer seasons. A thorough review may include:

- **Updating the OSHA 300 Log.** Ensure that all 2024 recordable incidents have been properly logged and that the 300A summary currently posted is accurate.
- **Reviewing the Injury and Illness Prevention Program (IIPP).** Employers in states that require written IIPPs (such as California) should review and update their plans. Even where not required by state law, maintaining a written safety program is a best practice.
- **Conducting workplace inspections.** Walk through the workplace to identify hazards such as blocked exits, damaged equipment, inadequate lighting, or slip and trip hazards. Document findings and corrective actions.
- **Refreshing safety training.** Ensure that employees have received required training on topics such as hazard communication, fire safety, emergency procedures, and any industry-specific hazards. New employees should receive training promptly.
- **Reviewing workers' compensation claims.** Analyze claim trends from the prior year to identify patterns that may suggest recurring hazards or areas where additional controls are needed.

Employers in industries with seasonal hazards---such as construction, agriculture, and outdoor services---should pay particular attention to warm-weather risks including heat illness, which is subject to specific OSHA standards in some states and has been the focus of a proposed federal standard.

## Q2 Planning Considerations

As employers look ahead to the second quarter, several planning priorities stand out:

**Mid-year benefits check-in.** Q2 is a good time to assess how benefits programs are performing, review utilization data, and identify any compliance issues before the fall renewal season.

**Manager training.** Consider scheduling training for managers on topics such as performance management, leave administration, and anti-harassment. Well-trained managers are one of the most effective defenses against employment claims.

**Compliance calendar review.** Verify that all Q1 deadlines were met and update the compliance calendar for Q2. Key dates may include EEO-1 filing, state-specific reporting deadlines, and benefit plan notice requirements.

**Policy updates.** If state legislative changes enacted in Q1 require policy updates, plan for implementation and employee communication in Q2.

## Key Action Items

- **Audit hiring practices** for compliance with pay transparency, ban-the-box, and youth employment laws.
- **Prepare EEO-1 data** and register for the EEOC's online filing system if required.
- **Monitor state legislative sessions** for employment law developments affecting your operations.
- **Conduct a workplace safety program review** and address any gaps in training, documentation, or hazard controls.
- **Plan Q2 compliance priorities**, including manager training, benefits review, and policy updates.
- **Complete ACA electronic filing** with the IRS by March 31 if applicable.
