# What Employers Are Asking: February 2025

Below are several questions that employers have been raising in early 2025, along with general guidance. These responses are intended as starting points for employers to consider; specific situations should be discussed with qualified legal, tax, or HR advisors.

## Q: When does the OSHA 300A posting requirement begin, and what exactly must we post?

Employers who are required to maintain OSHA injury and illness records must post the OSHA Form 300A, the Summary of Work-Related Injuries and Illnesses, from **February 1 through April 30** each year. The posted summary must cover the prior calendar year---in this case, 2024.

The Form 300A is a summary document that includes totals for the number of cases, days away from work, days of job transfer or restriction, and other recorded information. It does not include individual employee names. A company executive must certify the form by signing it before it is posted.

The summary must be displayed in a conspicuous place where notices to employees are customarily posted. For employers with remote workers, best practice may include electronic distribution or posting on an intranet, though OSHA's guidance on remote posting continues to evolve.

Employers with fewer than 11 employees during the previous calendar year, or those in certain low-hazard industries, may be partially exempt from OSHA recordkeeping requirements. However, all employers must report severe incidents (fatalities, hospitalizations, amputations, and losses of an eye) regardless of exemption status.

## Q: Has the IRS extended the deadline for furnishing 1095-C forms to employees?

Employers should plan against the statutory deadline unless the IRS has officially announced an extension. For the 2024 tax year, the statutory deadline for furnishing Form 1095-C to full-time employees is March 3, 2025 (because March 1 falls on a Saturday).

In several prior years, the IRS or Congress provided extensions to the employee furnishing deadline, sometimes with relatively short notice. However, relying on past extensions is not a safe compliance strategy. Employers should prepare to meet the statutory deadline and treat any announced extension as a welcome buffer rather than an assumed entitlement.

If an employer determines that it cannot meet the furnishing deadline, it may request an extension by submitting Form 8809, Application for Extension of Time to File Information Returns, though this extension applies to the IRS filing deadline rather than the employee furnishing deadline. The rules for extending the employee furnishing deadline are more limited, and employers in this situation should consult counsel or their ACA reporting vendor.

## Q: Can we implement a policy governing employee use of social media?

Employers may adopt social media policies, but those policies must be drafted carefully to avoid running afoul of several legal considerations:

**National Labor Relations Act (NLRA).** The National Labor Relations Board (NLRB) has taken the position that overly broad social media policies may chill employees' rights to engage in protected concerted activity under Section 7 of the NLRA. Policies that could be read to prohibit employees from discussing wages, working conditions, or workplace concerns with coworkers---even on personal social media accounts---may be found unlawful. This applies to most private-sector employers, not just those with unionized workforces.

**Anti-discrimination and anti-retaliation laws.** Employers should ensure that social media monitoring and enforcement is applied consistently and does not target employees based on protected characteristics. Additionally, employees who report safety concerns, discrimination, or other protected complaints through social media may be protected under whistleblower or anti-retaliation statutes.

**State privacy laws.** Several states have enacted laws restricting employers from requiring employees or applicants to provide access to personal social media accounts. Employers should be aware of these restrictions in the states where they operate.

A well-drafted social media policy may address legitimate concerns such as protecting confidential business information, prohibiting harassment, and reminding employees not to speak on behalf of the company without authorization. Employers should have such policies reviewed by counsel to ensure they are appropriately tailored.

## Q: What tax implications should we consider for employees working remotely in a different state?

Remote work arrangements where employees work in a state different from the employer's location can create several tax and compliance considerations:

**State income tax withholding.** Employers may be required to withhold state income tax in the state where the employee is physically working, even if the employer has no office in that state. Requirements vary, and some states have reciprocity agreements that may simplify multi-state withholding obligations.

**Nexus and business tax exposure.** Having an employee working in a state may create "nexus" for state business tax purposes, potentially subjecting the employer to corporate income tax, franchise tax, or other business taxes in that state. The standards for establishing nexus vary by state.

**Unemployment insurance.** Employers must generally pay state unemployment insurance (SUI) tax in the state where the employee performs services. If an employee relocates to a new state, the employer may need to register for SUI purposes in that state.

**Workers' compensation.** Employers typically need workers' compensation coverage in the state where the employee works. An employee working remotely from a different state may require the employer to obtain coverage in that state.

**Local tax obligations.** Some cities and counties impose local income taxes, business license requirements, or other obligations that may be triggered by an employee working within their jurisdiction.

Employers with remote workers in multiple states should consult with tax and employment law professionals to ensure compliance with each jurisdiction's requirements. A multi-state compliance review is advisable before approving remote work arrangements in new states.
