# Compliance Alert: FLSA Overtime Classification Requirements

Proper classification of employees as exempt or non-exempt under the Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA) remains one of the most significant compliance challenges for employers. Misclassification can result in substantial back pay liability, liquidated damages, and legal fees. This alert summarizes the current state of the law and outlines steps employers can take to reduce risk.

## Current Federal Salary Threshold

The FLSA requires that most employees receive overtime pay at one and one-half times their regular rate for hours worked beyond 40 in a workweek. Certain employees may be exempt from overtime if they meet both a salary basis test and a duties test for the executive, administrative, professional, computer employee, or outside sales exemptions.

As of early 2025, the federal salary threshold for the "white-collar" exemptions stands at $35,568 per year ($684 per week). This is the level established by the 2019 Department of Labor (DOL) final rule.

The DOL issued a new rule in 2024 that would have increased the threshold in two stages, ultimately to $58,656 per year. However, a federal district court in Texas vacated the 2024 rule in its entirety, reverting the threshold to the 2019 level. Employers should be aware that this legal landscape could shift through further court proceedings or new rulemaking by the DOL.

## State Salary Thresholds

Many states impose their own salary thresholds for overtime exemptions, and some of these are significantly higher than the federal level. For example:

- **California** generally ties its exemption salary threshold to twice the state minimum wage for full-time employment, which as of January 2025 exceeds the federal threshold.
- **New York** maintains salary thresholds that vary by region and have been increasing on a scheduled basis.
- **Washington, Colorado, and Maine** are among other states with salary thresholds that exceed the federal standard.

Employers must comply with whichever threshold---federal or state---is higher. Multi-state employers should verify the applicable threshold in each jurisdiction where they have exempt employees.

## The Duties Test

Meeting the salary threshold alone is not sufficient to establish an exemption. Each exempt classification requires that the employee's primary duty meet specific criteria:

- **Executive exemption:** The employee's primary duty must be management of the enterprise or a customarily recognized department, the employee must regularly direct the work of two or more other full-time employees, and the employee must have authority to hire or fire (or whose recommendations on such matters are given particular weight).
- **Administrative exemption:** The employee's primary duty must be the performance of office or non-manual work directly related to the management or general business operations of the employer, and the duty must include the exercise of discretion and independent judgment on matters of significance.
- **Professional exemption:** The employee's primary duty must require knowledge of an advanced type in a field of science or learning customarily acquired by a prolonged course of specialized intellectual instruction (learned professional) or must require invention, imagination, originality, or talent in a recognized field of artistic or creative endeavor (creative professional).

Employers commonly err by assuming that a job title or salary level alone determines exempt status. The actual duties performed by the employee are what matter, and those duties must be evaluated on a case-by-case basis.

## Common Misclassification Risks

Several patterns frequently give rise to misclassification claims:

- **Classifying employees as exempt based on title rather than duties.** A "manager" title does not establish an exemption if the employee spends most of their time performing non-managerial work.
- **Applying the administrative exemption too broadly.** The exercise of "discretion and independent judgment on matters of significance" is a high bar. Employees who follow detailed procedures or apply well-established standards may not meet this test.
- **Failing to account for state-specific rules.** Some states interpret exemption duties tests differently than the federal standard, or have additional exemption categories with their own requirements.
- **Misclassifying working supervisors.** In industries like retail, food service, and healthcare, front-line supervisors may spend a majority of their time on non-exempt tasks. Their classification should be evaluated based on their actual job duties across a representative period.

## Employer Audit Steps

Employers should consider conducting periodic self-audits of their exempt classifications. A reasonable audit process may include:

1. **Identify all employees currently classified as exempt** and the specific exemption applied to each.
2. **Verify salary levels** against both federal and applicable state thresholds.
3. **Review actual duties** performed by each exempt employee, not just the job description. Consider interviewing supervisors and employees to understand how time is actually spent.
4. **Compare duties to the applicable tests** for the claimed exemption under both federal and state law.
5. **Document the analysis** and rationale for each classification determination.
6. **Consult counsel** if there is uncertainty about whether an employee meets the requirements for exemption, particularly for positions that involve mixed duties.

Reclassifying employees from exempt to non-exempt requires careful planning, including communication to affected employees, implementation of timekeeping systems, and consideration of compensation adjustments. Employers should approach reclassification decisions with the guidance of experienced employment counsel.
