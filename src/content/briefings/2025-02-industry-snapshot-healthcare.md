# Industry Snapshot: Employment Challenges in Healthcare

Healthcare employers---including physician practices, dental offices, outpatient clinics, home health agencies, skilled nursing facilities, and behavioral health providers---face a distinct set of employment challenges. This snapshot examines several of the most pressing issues based on publicly available industry reporting and regulatory developments.

## Workforce Shortages and Recruitment Difficulties

Healthcare has experienced persistent workforce shortages across many roles, including registered nurses, medical assistants, home health aides, behavioral health professionals, and allied health workers. These shortages have been well-documented by the Bureau of Labor Statistics, state health workforce centers, and industry associations.

For small and mid-sized healthcare employers, competing for talent against large health systems with deeper resources can be particularly challenging. Strategies that employers have reported pursuing include:

- Expanding recruitment geographically and considering remote or hybrid roles for administrative positions
- Offering tuition assistance or student loan repayment support for clinical staff
- Developing pipeline programs with local educational institutions
- Reexamining entry-level role requirements to ensure that credential expectations are not unnecessarily limiting the applicant pool

Employers should ensure that any recruitment incentive programs are structured in a way that complies with applicable wage and hour laws, tax regulations, and---for certain healthcare roles---federal anti-kickback and self-referral rules.

## Burnout and Retention

Clinician and staff burnout continues to be a widely reported concern in healthcare. The Surgeon General issued an advisory on health worker burnout and well-being in 2022, and the issue has remained a focus of national discussion. Burnout contributes to turnover, which creates operational strain, increases recruitment costs, and may affect patient care quality.

Employers may consider reviewing workload distribution, scheduling practices, and administrative burdens on clinical staff. Some organizations have reported implementing peer support programs, employee assistance program (EAP) enhancements, and protected time for documentation. Addressing burnout is not only a retention strategy but may also be relevant to workplace safety and regulatory compliance, as fatigued workers may face higher injury risk.

## Scheduling and Wage-Hour Compliance

Healthcare operations often require around-the-clock staffing, variable shift lengths, and on-call arrangements. These scheduling realities create wage and hour compliance considerations that healthcare employers should monitor:

**Overtime calculations.** Healthcare employers should ensure overtime is calculated correctly, including for employees who work at multiple locations or in multiple roles at different pay rates. The "regular rate" calculation under the FLSA can be complex when it must account for shift differentials, bonuses, or on-call pay.

**On-call and standby time.** Whether on-call time constitutes compensable "hours worked" depends on factors including the degree to which the employee's freedom is restricted. Employers should review on-call policies and practices to confirm that they align with applicable federal and state standards.

**Meal and rest break compliance.** Many states require employers to provide meal and rest breaks, and some have specific provisions regarding healthcare workers. Employers should verify that their break policies satisfy state law requirements and that employees are actually able to take uninterrupted breaks.

**The 8/80 overtime system.** The FLSA permits certain healthcare employers to use an alternative overtime calculation---the "8 and 80" system---under which overtime is calculated based on 8 hours in a day or 80 hours in a 14-day period, rather than the standard 40-hour workweek. Employers who wish to use this system must meet specific requirements, including establishing the 14-day period in advance.

## OSHA Compliance: Bloodborne Pathogens and Beyond

Healthcare employers are subject to a range of OSHA standards, with the Bloodborne Pathogens standard (29 CFR 1910.1030) being among the most significant. Key compliance obligations include maintaining an Exposure Control Plan, offering hepatitis B vaccination to exposed employees, using sharps with engineered injury protections, maintaining a sharps injury log, and providing annual training.

Beyond bloodborne pathogens, healthcare employers should also attend to:

- **Workplace violence prevention.** OSHA has issued guidance on preventing workplace violence in healthcare settings, and some states, including California, have enacted workplace violence prevention laws with specific healthcare provisions. Healthcare workers experience workplace violence at rates significantly higher than most other industries.
- **Ergonomic hazards.** Patient handling and repositioning activities are a leading cause of musculoskeletal injuries among healthcare workers. Employers may consider safe patient handling programs and equipment to reduce injury risk.
- **Respiratory protection.** Employers who require employees to use respirators must comply with OSHA's Respiratory Protection standard, including fit testing, medical evaluations, and training requirements.

## Credentialing and Licensure Verification

Healthcare employers are generally expected to verify and maintain current records of employee professional licenses, certifications, and credentials. Requirements vary by state and by the type of facility or practice. Failure to ensure that clinical staff hold current credentials can create regulatory risk, liability exposure, and potential patient safety concerns.

Employers should establish systems for tracking credential expiration dates and initiating reverification well in advance of expiration. Some states require primary source verification rather than reliance on employee self-reporting.

## Looking Ahead

Healthcare employers may benefit from staying attentive to developments in telehealth staffing regulations, state-level scope-of-practice expansions, and evolving workplace safety standards. As the regulatory environment continues to change, proactive compliance monitoring and regular policy reviews can help healthcare employers manage risk while supporting their workforce.
