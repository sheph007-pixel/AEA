# What Employers Are Asking: January 2025

Each month, we compile questions that employers frequently raise at the start of the year. Below are several topics that have been generating discussion, along with general guidance. As always, employers should consult qualified counsel for advice on their specific circumstances.

## Q: What are the key compliance deadlines in Q1 2025?

The first quarter includes several important deadlines that employers should track:

- **January 31:** Deadline for furnishing Form W-2 to employees and filing Forms W-2 with the Social Security Administration (if filing electronically). Also the deadline for furnishing Form 1099-NEC to independent contractors.
- **February 1 through April 30:** OSHA Form 300A (Summary of Work-Related Injuries and Illnesses) must be posted in the workplace from February 1 through April 30 for employers required to maintain OSHA injury and illness records.
- **March 2 (or applicable deadline):** OSHA electronic submission of injury and illness data, for covered employers.
- **March 3 (statutory deadline for 1095-C furnishing):** Applicable Large Employers must furnish Form 1095-C to full-time employees. Employers should monitor IRS announcements for any extensions, but should plan against the statutory deadline.
- **March 31:** Deadline for electronic filing of Forms 1094-C and 1095-C with the IRS (if filing electronically).

Employers should build a compliance calendar at the start of each year and assign responsibility for each deadline to avoid oversights.

## Q: What is the current status of the federal salary threshold for overtime exemptions?

The federal overtime exemption landscape has been in flux. In 2024, the Department of Labor (DOL) issued a final rule that would have raised the salary threshold for the "white-collar" exemptions under the Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA) in two phases. The first increase, to $43,888 per year, took effect on July 1, 2024. A second increase, to $58,656 per year, was scheduled for January 1, 2025.

However, a federal court in Texas vacated the 2024 rule in its entirety in late 2024, which had the effect of reverting the federal salary threshold to the prior level of $35,568 per year (or $684 per week). Employers should note that this legal landscape may continue to evolve through appeals or new rulemaking.

Importantly, many states maintain their own salary thresholds for overtime exemptions that exceed the federal level. Employers must comply with whichever threshold---federal or state---is most favorable to the employee. Employers should verify the applicable thresholds in each state where they have exempt employees and consult counsel if they are uncertain about classification decisions.

## Q: What are our obligations when we close the workplace due to winter weather?

Winter weather closures raise several pay-related questions. The answer depends on whether affected employees are classified as exempt or non-exempt under the FLSA and applicable state law.

**Exempt employees.** Under federal law, exempt employees generally must receive their full salary for any workweek in which they perform any work. If the employer closes the business for a full day due to weather, it may deduct a full day from an exempt employee's salary only if the employee performs no work that day and the deduction is made under the employer's bona fide paid time off policy. Deductions that do not fit within the recognized exceptions may jeopardize the employee's exempt status.

**Non-exempt employees.** Non-exempt employees generally need to be paid only for hours actually worked. If the employer closes and non-exempt employees do not work, there is typically no federal obligation to pay for the time not worked. However, some state laws, "reporting time" pay requirements, or employer policies may require compensation in certain closure situations.

Employers should review their policies, applicable state reporting-time pay laws, and any collective bargaining agreements before making pay decisions related to weather closures.

## Q: Have there been updates to new hire paperwork requirements?

Employers should verify that they are using current versions of required new hire forms:

- **Form I-9.** U.S. Citizenship and Immigration Services (USCIS) periodically updates Form I-9, Employment Eligibility Verification. Employers should confirm they are using the edition that is currently valid and accepted. The form edition date appears in the lower left corner of the document. Using an expired version may result in penalties during an audit.
- **Form W-4.** The IRS releases an updated Form W-4 annually. While employers are not required to have existing employees complete a new W-4 each year, all new hires should complete the current version.
- **State-specific forms.** Many states require their own withholding forms, new hire reporting forms, and wage notices at the time of hire. Requirements vary significantly by state, and employers should ensure their onboarding packets include all state-mandated documents for the jurisdictions where employees will work.

Employers should review onboarding checklists at least annually to ensure they reflect current requirements and form versions.

## Q: Are there any changes to state new hire reporting requirements?

All states require employers to report new hires and rehires to a designated state agency, typically within 20 days of the employee's start date, though some states have shorter windows. This reporting is used to assist in the enforcement of child support orders and other purposes.

Employers should confirm the reporting deadline and method for each state where they have employees. Multi-state employers should be especially attentive, as reporting requirements and timelines vary. Failure to comply with new hire reporting obligations may result in penalties, though enforcement practices differ by state.
