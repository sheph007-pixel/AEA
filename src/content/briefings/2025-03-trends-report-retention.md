# Employer Trends: Retention Strategies That Are Working

Employee retention remains a top concern for employers of all sizes. While turnover rates vary by industry and region, the costs associated with replacing an employee---recruiting, onboarding, lost productivity, and institutional knowledge drain---are consistently significant. This report examines retention strategies that employers have been adopting, based on general industry observations and publicly available research.

## Flexible Work Arrangements

Flexibility has become one of the most discussed elements of the employment value proposition. Employers across a range of industries have been experimenting with various models:

**Remote and hybrid work.** For roles where remote work is feasible, many employers have settled into hybrid arrangements that balance in-office collaboration with remote flexibility. The specific structure varies widely---some employers designate certain days as in-office days, while others allow employees to choose. Employers that have rigidly mandated full-time return to office have, in some cases, reported increased voluntary turnover, particularly among experienced employees with in-demand skills.

**Flexible scheduling.** For roles that require on-site presence---such as manufacturing, healthcare, retail, and food service---employers have explored other forms of flexibility, including compressed workweeks, shift-swapping platforms, self-scheduling within guardrails, and predictable scheduling commitments. These approaches recognize that flexibility is not synonymous with remote work.

**Results-oriented management.** Some employers have shifted toward evaluating employees based on output and results rather than hours logged or physical presence. This approach can support retention by demonstrating trust, though it requires clear performance expectations and communication.

Employers should ensure that any flexible work arrangement complies with applicable wage and hour laws, including proper tracking of hours worked for non-exempt employees and attention to multi-state tax and employment law obligations for remote workers.

## Career Development and Internal Mobility

Employees frequently cite lack of growth opportunity as a reason for leaving an organization. Employers have been responding with various development-oriented strategies:

**Clear advancement pathways.** Employers who have defined and communicated career ladders or progression frameworks report that employees have greater clarity about their future within the organization. This is particularly relevant for mid-sized employers, where career paths may be less obvious than in larger organizations with more hierarchical structures.

**Skills-based development.** Rather than limiting development to traditional promotions, some employers are investing in skills training, cross-functional projects, and stretch assignments that allow employees to grow their capabilities. This approach can be particularly effective in flatter organizations where upward mobility may be limited.

**Tuition assistance and credentialing support.** Employer-sponsored education benefits---including tuition reimbursement, certification funding, and partnerships with educational institutions---continue to be valued by employees. Some employers have expanded these programs to include non-degree credentials, online courses, and professional development stipends.

**Internal job postings.** Making internal opportunities visible and accessible can help retain employees who might otherwise look externally. Employers who post open positions internally before or concurrently with external postings signal that they value internal talent.

## Benefits Improvements

Compensation remains important, but employers are also finding that targeted benefits improvements can meaningfully affect retention:

**Mental health and well-being support.** Expanded access to mental health services, employee assistance programs, and well-being initiatives has become a common retention strategy. Employers have reported strengthening EAP offerings, adding mental health days, and training managers to recognize signs of employee distress.

**Financial wellness programs.** Programs that address financial stress---such as emergency savings facilitation, student loan repayment assistance, and financial literacy education---are gaining traction. These benefits may be particularly relevant for younger employees or those in lower-wage roles.

**Caregiving support.** Benefits that support employees with caregiving responsibilities---including dependent care flexible spending accounts, backup childcare, and elder care resources---can help retain employees who might otherwise reduce hours or leave the workforce.

**Voluntary benefits.** Expanding the menu of voluntary benefits (such as pet insurance, identity theft protection, legal services, and supplemental insurance) at no or low cost to the employer can enhance the overall benefits package and improve employee satisfaction.

Employers should review benefit offerings periodically to ensure they remain competitive and aligned with workforce needs. Employee surveys, exit interview data, and benefits utilization reports can inform these assessments.

## Manager Training and Development

Research consistently indicates that the relationship between an employee and their direct manager is one of the strongest predictors of retention. Employers are investing in manager effectiveness through several approaches:

**First-time manager training.** Many employees are promoted into management roles without formal training in people management. Providing structured training on topics such as feedback delivery, performance management, conflict resolution, and legal compliance can improve manager effectiveness and reduce turnover on their teams.

**Ongoing coaching and support.** Rather than treating manager training as a one-time event, some employers provide ongoing support through peer learning groups, coaching, and regular check-ins with HR.

**Accountability for retention outcomes.** Some employers are incorporating retention metrics or employee engagement scores into manager performance evaluations, signaling that people management is a core part of the role.

**Communication skills development.** Training managers to have effective one-on-one meetings, conduct stay interviews (conversations aimed at understanding what keeps employees engaged), and respond to employee concerns can strengthen the manager-employee relationship.

## Putting It Together

There is no single retention strategy that works for every employer or every employee. The most effective approaches tend to combine multiple elements---competitive compensation, meaningful benefits, flexibility, growth opportunities, and strong management---tailored to the specific needs and demographics of the workforce.

Employers who regularly assess their retention drivers, listen to employee feedback, and act on what they learn are generally better positioned to retain the talent they need. The cost of thoughtful retention investment is typically far less than the cost of persistent turnover.
