# Industry Snapshot: Retail Employer Compliance Considerations

Retail employers face a compliance landscape shaped by high turnover, variable scheduling, a significant proportion of hourly workers, and a rapidly evolving patchwork of state and local regulations. This snapshot examines several of the most important compliance areas for retail businesses.

## Predictive Scheduling Laws

A growing number of cities and states have enacted "fair workweek" or predictive scheduling laws that impose requirements on how retail (and food service) employers schedule their workers. While the specifics vary by jurisdiction, common provisions include:

**Advance notice of schedules.** Employers may be required to provide work schedules a certain number of days in advance---typically 7 to 14 days, depending on the jurisdiction. Changes made after the schedule is posted may trigger "predictability pay" premiums owed to affected employees.

**Right to rest.** Some ordinances restrict "clopening" shifts---where an employee closes the business at night and opens it the next morning---by requiring a minimum number of hours between shifts or imposing premium pay for short turnarounds.

**Offer of additional hours.** Before hiring new employees, covered employers may be required to offer additional hours to existing part-time workers who have expressed interest in more hours.

**Right to request schedule changes.** Some laws give employees the right to request changes to their schedule without retaliation.

Jurisdictions with predictive scheduling laws include, among others, the cities of San Francisco, Seattle, New York City, Chicago, Philadelphia, and Los Angeles, as well as the state of Oregon. Retail employers operating in or expanding into these areas should review their scheduling practices and systems for compliance.

## Tip Pooling and Tip Credit

Retail employers whose workers receive tips---common in certain retail segments such as salons, spas, and hospitality-adjacent retail---must navigate complex rules around tip handling:

**Federal tip credit.** Under the FLSA, employers may take a "tip credit," paying tipped employees a reduced direct cash wage (currently $2.13 per hour at the federal level) provided that tips bring the employee's total compensation to at least the full minimum wage. However, many states have eliminated or restricted the tip credit, requiring employers to pay a higher direct cash wage or the full minimum wage before tips.

**Tip pooling.** Federal rules permit mandatory tip pooling among employees who customarily and regularly receive tips. Under current DOL regulations, when an employer does not take a tip credit, it may include a broader range of employees in the tip pool, including back-of-house workers, subject to certain restrictions. Employers should review both federal and state law, as state rules on tip pooling vary significantly.

**Management participation.** Managers and supervisors are generally prohibited from participating in tip pools. The definition of "manager" or "supervisor" for this purpose can be nuanced, particularly for working supervisors who spend significant time on non-supervisory duties.

Retail employers who handle tips should audit their practices regularly to ensure compliance with the applicable federal and state requirements.

## Seasonal and Temporary Workers

Many retail employers rely on seasonal or temporary workers to staff peak periods such as the holiday season, back-to-school, and summer months. Compliance considerations for these workers include:

**Wage and hour obligations.** Seasonal and temporary workers are entitled to the same minimum wage and overtime protections as permanent employees. Employers should ensure that these workers are included in all timekeeping and payroll compliance processes.

**Benefits eligibility.** Employers should understand how seasonal workers affect benefit plan eligibility determinations, including ACA full-time employee calculations. The ACA provides a limited exception for seasonal workers in determining ALE status, but the rules are specific and should be applied carefully.

**Onboarding compliance.** Form I-9 must be completed for every new hire, including seasonal rehires if more than three years have elapsed since the original form was completed. State new hire reporting requirements also apply to seasonal workers.

**Unemployment insurance.** Seasonal workers may be eligible for unemployment benefits at the end of their employment period. Employers should factor potential unemployment claims into their workforce planning.

## Youth Employment Restrictions

Retail is one of the largest employers of minors, and federal and state child labor laws impose significant restrictions that retail employers must follow:

**Hours limitations.** The FLSA restricts the number of hours that 14- and 15-year-olds may work, including limits on hours per day, hours per week, and time-of-day restrictions. During the school year, these restrictions are tighter. Sixteen- and 17-year-olds have fewer hours restrictions but are still subject to hazardous work prohibitions.

**Prohibited tasks.** Federal law prohibits minors under 18 from performing certain hazardous occupations. For retail employers, the most commonly relevant restrictions involve operating power-driven machinery (including certain food slicers, balers, and compactors) and driving motor vehicles. State laws may impose additional restrictions.

**Work permits and age certificates.** Many states require minors to obtain work permits or employment certificates before beginning work. Employers should verify that required documentation has been obtained and retained.

**State variations.** State child labor laws frequently go beyond federal requirements, with stricter hours limitations, additional prohibited occupations, and mandatory break requirements for minors. Employers should comply with the most restrictive applicable standard.

The Department of Labor has increased enforcement of child labor violations in recent years, and penalties for violations can be substantial---particularly for repeat offenders or violations involving hazardous work.

## Wage and Hour Risks

Retail operations present several recurring wage and hour risks that employers should actively manage:

**Off-the-clock work.** Requiring or permitting employees to perform work before clocking in or after clocking out---such as setting up displays, restocking, or closing out registers---creates liability for unpaid wages and overtime. Employers should establish clear policies prohibiting off-the-clock work and train managers to enforce them.

**Meal and rest break compliance.** Many states require that retail employees receive meal and rest breaks of specified duration. In some states, failure to provide compliant breaks results in premium pay obligations. Employers should build break periods into schedules and track whether breaks are actually taken.

**Rounding practices.** Some employers round employee time entries to the nearest increment (e.g., quarter-hour). While federal law permits neutral rounding practices, the trend in some states---notably California---has moved toward requiring payment for all time worked without rounding. Employers should review whether their rounding practices remain lawful in each applicable jurisdiction.

**Commission and bonus calculations in overtime.** Retail employees who earn commissions or bonuses may be entitled to have those amounts included in their regular rate of pay for overtime calculation purposes. Employers should verify that their overtime calculations comply with FLSA and state law requirements.

Retail employers benefit from regular wage and hour audits, manager training on timekeeping and scheduling compliance, and clear written policies that address the industry's most common risk areas.
