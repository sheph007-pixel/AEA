## A Dramatic Shift

For decades, noncompete agreements were a standard tool employers used to protect trade secrets, client relationships, and competitive advantages. That era is ending. A growing number of states have banned or severely restricted noncompetes for most employees, and the Federal Trade Commission (FTC) proposed a near-total ban on noncompetes in January 2023.

Whether or not the federal ban takes effect, the direction is clear. Employers need to understand the current restrictions and develop alternative strategies for protecting legitimate business interests.

## State-Level Restrictions

The state landscape varies widely, but the trend is toward restriction:

**Complete bans.** California has long prohibited noncompetes, and courts there will not enforce them regardless of where they were signed. Oklahoma and North Dakota have similar prohibitions.

**Income-based thresholds.** Several states, including Colorado, Illinois, Maine, Maryland, New Hampshire, Oregon, Rhode Island, Virginia, and Washington, prohibit noncompetes for employees earning below specified thresholds, often targeting lower-wage workers.

**Specific worker protections.** Some states ban noncompetes for particular categories of workers, such as hourly employees, healthcare professionals, or broadcast employees.

**Notice and consideration requirements.** Many states that still allow noncompetes impose procedural requirements, such as advance notice before employment begins, separate consideration for existing employees, or a garden leave provision requiring continued compensation during the restricted period.

## The FTC Proposed Rule

The FTC's proposed rule, if finalized, would prohibit most noncompete clauses nationwide and require employers to rescind existing ones. The proposal includes a limited exception for noncompetes entered in connection with the sale of a business. Legal challenges to the FTC's authority to issue such a rule are likely, and the outcome is uncertain.

Regardless of the final outcome, the proposal signals that federal enforcement interest in noncompetes is high, and employers relying heavily on noncompetes should be preparing alternatives.

## Alternative Protective Strategies

**Nondisclosure agreements (NDAs).** Agreements that protect specific confidential information and trade secrets are generally enforceable and are not affected by noncompete bans. Ensure your NDAs clearly define what constitutes confidential information and are tailored to the employee's actual access.

**Non-solicitation agreements.** Agreements that prohibit former employees from soliciting your clients or recruiting your employees are typically viewed more favorably than noncompetes because they are narrower in scope. However, some jurisdictions restrict these as well, so review applicable law.

**Invention assignment agreements.** For employees involved in creating intellectual property, ensure clear assignment of work product through well-drafted IP agreements.

**Trade secret protections.** The federal Defend Trade Secrets Act and state trade secret laws (most based on the Uniform Trade Secrets Act) provide legal remedies for misappropriation of trade secrets regardless of whether a noncompete exists. Strengthen your trade secret protections by identifying and marking confidential materials, limiting access on a need-to-know basis, using strong IT security controls, and conducting exit interviews that remind departing employees of their continuing obligations.

**Garden leave clauses.** If your jurisdiction allows them, garden leave provisions require continued compensation during the restricted period, making noncompetes more likely to be enforced and more fair to the employee.

**Retention strategies.** The best protection against a departing employee harming your business is giving them a reason to stay. Competitive compensation, strong culture, growth opportunities, and equity participation reduce the risk that your most valuable employees leave for a competitor.

## Action Items for Employers

**1. Audit your existing agreements.** Review every noncompete, non-solicitation, and NDA in your organization. Determine which are enforceable under current law in each applicable jurisdiction.

**2. Stop using noncompetes where prohibited.** If you have employees in jurisdictions that ban noncompetes, stop requiring them for those employees immediately.

**3. Shift to alternative protections.** Begin transitioning from noncompetes to NDAs, non-solicitation agreements, and stronger trade secret protections.

**4. Strengthen your exit process.** Conduct thorough exit interviews that include reminders about confidentiality obligations, return of company property, and preservation of company data.

**5. Monitor legal developments.** The landscape is changing rapidly. Stay current on developments in every jurisdiction where you operate.
