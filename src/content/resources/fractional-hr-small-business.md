## What Is Fractional HR?

A fractional HR professional is a senior HR leader who works with your company on a part-time or contract basis, typically dedicating a set number of hours per week or month. This model gives small and growing businesses access to experienced HR leadership, including VP-level and CHRO-level expertise, at a fraction of the cost of a full-time senior hire.

For employers with 20 to 200 employees, fractional HR often hits the sweet spot: the business has outgrown informal, ad-hoc HR management but does not yet have the volume or budget to justify a full-time senior HR leader.

## When Fractional HR Makes Sense

Consider fractional HR if:

- Your HR function is currently handled by an office manager, bookkeeper, or the business owner, and compliance gaps are growing
- You have an HR coordinator or generalist who handles day-to-day tasks but lacks strategic experience
- You are preparing for rapid growth and need HR infrastructure built before scaling
- You face a specific challenge such as a compliance audit, handbook overhaul, or leadership transition
- You are expanding to new states and need someone who understands multi-state employment law
- You need an HR leader to develop and implement people strategy but cannot justify $150,000+ for a full-time VP of HR

## What Fractional HR Professionals Do

A fractional HR leader typically focuses on strategic and high-impact activities rather than daily administrative tasks. Common responsibilities include:

**Compliance and risk management.** Auditing current practices, identifying gaps, and implementing compliant policies, handbooks, and procedures.

**People strategy.** Developing talent acquisition, retention, compensation, and performance management strategies aligned with business goals.

**HR infrastructure.** Selecting and implementing HRIS, payroll, and benefits platforms. Establishing processes and workflows that will scale with the business.

**Leadership coaching.** Advising the CEO and management team on people-related decisions, difficult employee situations, and organizational design.

**Investigations and sensitive matters.** Handling complex employee relations issues, investigations, and terminations with the expertise needed to manage legal risk.

**Project-based work.** Designing compensation structures, conducting pay equity analyses, preparing for due diligence in an acquisition, or building out a performance management program.

## How It Typically Works

**Time commitment.** Most fractional engagements range from 8 to 40 hours per month, depending on the complexity of your needs. Some fractional HR professionals work with multiple clients simultaneously.

**Duration.** Engagements may be ongoing (month-to-month or annual contracts) or project-based (three to six months for a specific initiative). Many start as project-based and evolve into ongoing relationships.

**Cost.** Fractional HR typically costs between $3,000 and $12,000 per month, depending on hours and seniority. Compare this to the fully loaded cost of a full-time HR director ($120,000-$200,000+ including salary, benefits, and overhead).

**Integration.** Effective fractional HR leaders integrate with your team rather than operating as external consultants. They attend key meetings, build relationships with managers, and become familiar with your culture and operations.

## Finding the Right Fit

**Look for relevant experience.** Prioritize candidates with experience in your industry, your company size range, and the specific challenges you face. A fractional HR leader who has scaled companies from 30 to 150 employees brings different value than one who spent their career in Fortune 500 organizations.

**Check references.** Speak with other small business owners who have worked with the candidate. Ask about responsiveness, practical value, and cultural fit.

**Define the scope clearly.** Before engaging, agree on specific deliverables, time commitments, and priorities. Ambiguous engagements produce ambiguous results.

**Establish communication rhythms.** Set regular check-in schedules, reporting expectations, and escalation procedures so the relationship functions smoothly despite the part-time nature of the arrangement.

## Transitioning to Full-Time

As your business grows, you may eventually need a full-time HR leader. A good fractional HR professional can help with this transition by defining the role, building the infrastructure the full-time hire will manage, and even helping recruit their replacement.

Some fractional relationships last for years because the model continues to fit the business's needs. Others evolve naturally into full-time roles or transition to advisory relationships. The flexibility is one of the model's greatest advantages.

Fractional HR is not a compromise. For the right stage of business, it is the optimal model for accessing the HR expertise your organization needs.
