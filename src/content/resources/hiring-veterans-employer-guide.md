## The Business Case for Hiring Veterans

Military veterans bring a combination of leadership experience, discipline, adaptability, and technical training that translates directly to civilian employment. Yet many employers struggle to connect with veteran talent or to translate military experience into civilian job qualifications. This guide provides practical steps for building an effective veteran hiring program.

## Understanding Military Experience

One of the biggest barriers to hiring veterans is the translation gap between military and civilian terminology. A veteran's resume may describe responsibilities in military language that obscures highly relevant experience.

**Learn the basics.** Familiarize yourself with military rank structures, common military occupational specialties, and the general organization of military units. Online tools like the Military Skills Translator (available through the Department of Labor) can help convert military job codes to civilian equivalents.

**Look beyond job titles.** A military logistics specialist may have managed supply chains worth millions of dollars. A squad leader may have supervised teams under high-pressure conditions. An intelligence analyst may have synthesized complex data to support strategic decisions. Focus on the skills and responsibilities, not the military terminology.

**Value leadership experience.** Military service members receive leadership training and experience far earlier in their careers than most civilian workers. A 25-year-old veteran may have years of experience leading teams, managing operations, and making decisions under pressure.

## Recruitment Strategies

**Partner with veteran service organizations.** Organizations like Hire Heroes USA, American Corporate Partners, and local Veterans Affairs offices connect transitioning service members with employers. State workforce agencies often have veteran-specific employment programs.

**Attend military job fairs.** Career fairs at military installations and veteran-focused hiring events provide direct access to transitioning service members and recently separated veterans.

**Post on veteran-focused job boards.** Sites like Hire Heroes USA, Military.com, and RecruitMilitary reach veteran candidates specifically. Include veteran-friendly language in your postings.

**Engage with Guard and Reserve units.** National Guard and Reserve members are part-time military and part-time civilian. Building relationships with local units can create a pipeline of candidates.

## Adapting Your Hiring Process

**Review job requirements.** Ensure that degree requirements do not unnecessarily exclude veterans whose military training and experience provide equivalent or superior preparation. Many military training programs are rigorous and translate to civilian certifications.

**Train your interviewers.** Ensure hiring managers understand how to evaluate military experience and do not inadvertently penalize veterans for answering questions in a military communication style (which tends to be more structured and direct than typical civilian interview norms).

**Consider military credentials.** Many military occupations include training that leads to or is equivalent to civilian certifications. Work with your industry's certification body to understand military training equivalencies.

## Legal Protections: USERRA

The Uniformed Services Employment and Reemployment Rights Act (USERRA) provides important protections for employees who serve in the military:

- **Reemployment rights**: Employees who leave civilian employment for military service have the right to return to their position (or a comparable one) after service, provided they meet certain conditions.
- **Non-discrimination**: Employers cannot deny employment, reemployment, promotion, or any benefit of employment based on military service.
- **Health insurance continuation**: Employees on military leave have the right to continue employer-sponsored health insurance for up to 24 months.

USERRA applies to all employers regardless of size. Understanding these obligations is essential before hiring employees who are current or potential military service members.

## Retention and Integration

**Provide a structured onboarding.** Veterans are accustomed to clear orientation processes and well-defined expectations. A haphazard onboarding experience can be disorienting.

**Assign a mentor.** Pair new veteran hires with an experienced employee, ideally another veteran if one is available, who can help them navigate the organizational culture and unwritten rules of civilian workplaces.

**Be aware of transition challenges.** Some veterans face challenges adjusting to civilian workplace culture, including differences in communication style, hierarchy, and decision-making processes. Patience and open communication help.

**Create a veteran employee resource group.** For larger employers, a veteran ERG provides community, peer support, and a channel for raising concerns specific to military-connected employees.

Hiring veterans is both a service to those who served and a smart business decision. The key is bridging the gap between military and civilian employment through intentional recruitment, informed hiring practices, and supportive integration.
