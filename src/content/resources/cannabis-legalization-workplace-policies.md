As more states legalize recreational and medical marijuana, employers face increasing complexity in maintaining workplace drug policies. The patchwork of state laws, the disconnect between federal and state legal status, and evolving employee protections require employers to regularly review and update their approach.

## The Current Legal Landscape

### Federal law
Marijuana remains a Schedule I controlled substance under the federal Controlled Substances Act. This means:

- Federal contractors and employers in federally regulated industries (transportation, defense, nuclear energy) generally must maintain drug-free workplace programs that include marijuana
- Drug testing for marijuana is required for safety-sensitive positions regulated by the Department of Transportation (DOT), regardless of state law
- Employers receiving federal grants may have drug-free workplace obligations under the Drug-Free Workplace Act of 1988

### State legalization status
The majority of states have legalized marijuana in some form:

- A growing number of states have legalized recreational use for adults
- The vast majority of states have medical marijuana programs
- Legalization status changes frequently, so employers should verify the current law in every state where they have employees

### Employee protections
This is where complexity increases significantly. Legalization does not necessarily mean employers must tolerate marijuana use, but an increasing number of states have enacted laws that limit employer actions:

**Off-duty use protections:**
- Several states prohibit employers from taking adverse action against employees for lawful off-duty marijuana use
- These laws generally still allow employers to take action for on-the-job impairment or use during work hours
- California, Montana, New York, and other states have enacted explicit protections for off-duty recreational use

**Medical marijuana protections:**
- Many states with medical marijuana programs require employers to accommodate medical marijuana use to some degree
- Some states prohibit refusing to hire or terminating employees solely based on a positive marijuana test if they are registered medical marijuana patients
- Protections typically do not extend to use during work hours or impairment on the job
- Safety-sensitive positions and federally regulated employers are usually exempt

**Pre-employment testing restrictions:**
- A growing number of jurisdictions restrict or prohibit pre-employment marijuana testing
- New York City, Philadelphia, and several other cities prohibit pre-employment marijuana testing for most positions
- Nevada prohibits employers from refusing to hire based on a pre-employment marijuana test (with exceptions for safety-sensitive positions)

## Updating Your Drug Policy

### Step 1: Know your legal obligations
- Identify federal requirements that apply to your industry and specific positions
- Review the laws of every state and locality where you have employees
- Determine which positions qualify as safety-sensitive under applicable law
- Consult with employment counsel who is current on your jurisdiction's marijuana employment laws

### Step 2: Define your approach
Employers generally choose from several approaches:

**Maintain zero tolerance:** Continue testing for and prohibiting all marijuana use. This approach is simplest but increasingly difficult to sustain in states with employee protections and in competitive labor markets where it shrinks the applicant pool.

**Test for impairment, not use:** Focus enforcement on on-the-job impairment rather than off-duty use. This aligns with most employee protection laws but is complicated by the limitations of current testing technology.

**Eliminate marijuana from testing panels:** Remove marijuana from pre-employment and random drug testing while retaining it for reasonable suspicion and post-accident testing. Some employers take this approach to avoid losing candidates in legal-use states.

**Position-specific policies:** Maintain strict testing for safety-sensitive positions while relaxing requirements for positions that do not involve safety risks. This is the most nuanced approach and often the most defensible.

### Step 3: Address the impairment challenge
The fundamental challenge is that current marijuana testing cannot reliably determine present impairment:

- Standard urine tests detect THC metabolites that may remain in the system for days or weeks after use, long after any impairing effects have worn off
- Oral fluid (saliva) tests have a shorter detection window and may be a better proxy for recent use
- No test currently available provides the equivalent of a blood alcohol concentration measurement for marijuana
- Employers must rely on observable signs of impairment in combination with testing when reasonable suspicion exists

### Step 4: Train supervisors
Managers need training on:

- Your updated drug and alcohol policy
- How to recognize signs of impairment (not signs of marijuana use generally, but signs of on-the-job impairment)
- How to document observations of impairment objectively and factually
- When and how to initiate reasonable suspicion testing
- The importance of consistent, non-discriminatory application of the policy

## Practical Policy Elements

Your updated policy should address:

- **Prohibited conduct:** Clearly state that being impaired at work, using marijuana during work hours, and possessing marijuana on company premises remain prohibited regardless of state legalization
- **Testing circumstances:** Define when testing will occur (pre-employment, random, reasonable suspicion, post-accident) and which substances are tested
- **Accommodation process:** For states requiring medical marijuana accommodation, describe the process for requesting an accommodation and how it will be evaluated
- **Consequences:** Specify the consequences for policy violations, consistent with applicable law
- **Safety-sensitive positions:** Define which positions are designated safety-sensitive and any additional requirements for those roles
- **Federal compliance:** Note any federal requirements that apply to certain positions regardless of state law

## Impact on Hiring

In a tight labor market, marijuana testing policies directly affect your candidate pool:

- Some employers have seen applicant volumes increase after removing marijuana from pre-employment panels
- For non-safety-sensitive positions, the business case for pre-employment marijuana testing has weakened significantly
- Candidates in legal-use states increasingly expect employers to treat off-duty marijuana use like off-duty alcohol use

Balancing safety obligations, legal requirements, and competitive hiring realities requires a thoughtful, position-specific approach rather than a blanket policy applied uniformly across all roles.
