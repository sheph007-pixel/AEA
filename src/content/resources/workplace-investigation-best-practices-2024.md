## Investigations in a Changed Workplace

The fundamentals of workplace investigations have not changed: promptness, thoroughness, impartiality, and documentation remain the pillars. But the context has evolved. Remote and hybrid workforces create new challenges for conducting interviews and gathering evidence. Social media and messaging platforms generate new categories of evidence. And heightened awareness of retaliation has raised the stakes for how organizations handle complainants and witnesses.

## When to Investigate

Not every workplace complaint requires a formal investigation, but many more situations require one than employers realize. You should conduct a formal investigation when:

- An employee alleges harassment, discrimination, or retaliation
- There are allegations of policy violations that could lead to termination
- Multiple employees raise similar concerns about a manager or coworker
- There is evidence of theft, fraud, or safety violations
- An employee files a charge with the EEOC or a state agency

When in doubt, investigate. The cost of an unnecessary investigation is modest compared to the cost of failing to investigate a legitimate complaint.

## Selecting the Investigator

The investigator must be perceived as impartial. For complaints involving senior leadership or sensitive matters, consider using an external investigator. For other matters, an internal HR professional or trained manager can serve as investigator, provided they have no personal stake in the outcome and no reporting relationship to the parties involved.

Regardless of who conducts the investigation, they should be trained in investigation techniques, evidence evaluation, and relevant employment law.

## The Investigation Process

### Planning

Before conducting any interviews, review the complaint, identify the relevant policies, determine who should be interviewed, and plan the sequence of interviews. Draft an initial interview outline for each witness.

### Complainant Interview

Start with the complainant. Obtain a detailed account of the allegations, including dates, locations, witnesses, and any evidence they can provide. Ask open-ended questions and let them tell their story before probing for details. Explain the investigation process, the expectation of confidentiality (to the extent possible), and the prohibition against retaliation.

### Document and Evidence Collection

Gather relevant documents before interviewing the respondent. This may include emails, text messages, chat logs, performance records, schedules, surveillance footage, and social media posts.

For remote workplaces, relevant evidence increasingly includes Slack or Teams messages, video call recordings (where they exist and were lawfully made), and electronic documents. Work with IT to preserve and collect electronic evidence, ensuring you follow applicable privacy laws.

### Respondent Interview

Interview the respondent after reviewing available evidence. Present the allegations in general terms (enough for them to respond meaningfully) without revealing the identity of confidential witnesses unnecessarily. Allow them to provide their account and identify any witnesses or evidence that supports their position.

### Witness Interviews

Interview all identified witnesses and any additional witnesses discovered during the process. Ask neutral, open-ended questions. Do not lead witnesses toward a predetermined conclusion.

### Credibility Assessment

When accounts conflict, assess credibility based on: corroboration by other evidence or witnesses, consistency of the account, plausibility, motive, and demeanor. Document your credibility assessment and the factors you considered.

### Findings and Conclusions

Based on the evidence, reach a conclusion about whether the alleged conduct occurred and whether it violated company policy or law. Use a preponderance of the evidence standard (more likely than not) unless your policy specifies a different standard.

### Written Report

Prepare a written investigation report that documents the complaint, the evidence gathered, the interviews conducted, the factual findings, and the conclusion. The report should be factual and objective, avoiding legal conclusions or inflammatory language.

## Retaliation Prevention

Retaliation is the most frequently filed charge with the EEOC. Take concrete steps:

- Warn all parties and witnesses against retaliation at the outset
- Monitor the complainant's working conditions after the investigation
- Separate the complainant from the respondent during the investigation if necessary
- Document any employment decisions affecting the complainant or witnesses to demonstrate they were not retaliatory
- Follow up with the complainant periodically after the investigation concludes

## Closing the Investigation

Communicate the outcome to the complainant and the respondent. You do not need to disclose specific disciplinary actions taken against the respondent, but you should confirm that the matter was investigated, a conclusion was reached, and appropriate action was taken. Thank participants for their cooperation and reiterate the prohibition against retaliation.

A thorough, well-documented investigation protects your organization and your employees. It is one of the most important skills an HR professional can develop.
