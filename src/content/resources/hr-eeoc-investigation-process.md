## Receiving an EEOC Charge

When the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission receives a charge of discrimination, it notifies the employer by sending a copy of the charge along with information about the investigation process. The employer typically has 30 days to submit a position statement responding to the allegations. This initial response is critical and can significantly influence the direction of the investigation.

Upon receiving a charge, employers should immediately preserve all documents and electronic records related to the charging party, the employment decision at issue, and similarly situated employees. Implementing a litigation hold to prevent routine destruction of relevant records is essential.

## Drafting the Position Statement

The position statement is the employer's primary opportunity to present its version of events and supporting evidence. An effective position statement should address each allegation in the charge with specific facts, identify the legitimate, non-discriminatory reason for the employment action, provide documentary evidence supporting the employer's position, and identify witnesses with knowledge of the relevant events.

The statement should be factual and professional, avoiding personal attacks on the charging party. Since 2016, the EEOC has shared position statements with charging parties, who may submit a response. Employers should draft their statements with this disclosure in mind, ensuring accuracy and consistency with potential testimony.

## The Investigation Process

EEOC investigations may involve requests for additional information, witness interviews, on-site visits, and review of employer records. Employers are legally required to cooperate with EEOC investigations, and failure to do so can result in a subpoena or adverse inference.

During the investigation, the EEOC may request comparative data about how the employer treated other employees in similar situations. Having organized personnel files and consistent documentation practices greatly facilitates this process. Employers should designate a single point of contact for EEOC communications, typically the HR director or outside counsel.

## Mediation and Settlement

The EEOC offers mediation as an alternative to investigation. Mediation is voluntary, confidential, and can resolve charges more quickly and less expensively than a full investigation. Employers should seriously consider mediation, particularly when the facts are unfavorable or the cost of defense would exceed a reasonable settlement.

If mediation is unsuccessful or declined, the investigation continues. The EEOC will issue a determination of either reasonable cause or no reasonable cause. A no-cause finding effectively ends the EEOC process, though the charging party retains the right to file a private lawsuit. A cause finding leads to conciliation efforts between the EEOC and the employer.

## Post-Investigation Considerations

Regardless of the outcome, employers should use the experience to evaluate and improve their employment practices. Common areas for improvement include strengthening documentation of employment decisions, enhancing supervisor training on anti-discrimination laws, reviewing policies for potential disparate impact, and ensuring consistent application of workplace rules. Proactive compliance efforts reduce the likelihood of future charges and demonstrate good faith in the event of subsequent claims.
