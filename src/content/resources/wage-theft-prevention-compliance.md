## Wage Theft Is an Enforcement Priority

Wage theft, the failure to pay workers the wages they are legally owed, has become a major enforcement focus at both the federal and state level. State legislatures have been strengthening wage theft prevention laws, increasing penalties, and in some cases creating criminal liability for employers who fail to pay wages properly.

For employers, the term "wage theft" may sound extreme, but it covers common payroll errors that can result from ignorance or poor systems as easily as from intentional underpayment. Understanding your obligations is essential.

## Common Forms of Wage Theft

**Unpaid overtime.** Failing to pay non-exempt employees time-and-a-half for hours worked over 40 in a workweek, whether through misclassification, incorrect calculation, or off-the-clock work.

**Minimum wage violations.** Paying below the applicable federal, state, or local minimum wage, including failures to account for higher local minimums that may differ from the state rate.

**Off-the-clock work.** Requiring or permitting employees to perform work tasks before clocking in, after clocking out, or during unpaid meal breaks. This includes pre-shift setup, post-shift cleanup, and responding to emails or messages during off hours.

**Tip violations.** Failing to pay the correct tipped minimum wage, misapplying tip credits, or permitting illegal tip pooling that includes managers or owners.

**Missed meal and rest breaks.** In states that require paid rest breaks or premium pay for missed breaks, such as California, failing to provide these breaks or pay the associated premiums.

**Final pay delays.** Many states require that final wages be paid on the last day of employment or within a specified period. Late payment of final wages can trigger waiting time penalties.

**Improper deductions.** Making deductions from wages that are not authorized by law or by the employee, such as deductions for cash register shortages, damaged equipment, or uniforms that reduce pay below minimum wage.

## State Law Developments

Several trends are reshaping wage theft enforcement:

**Enhanced penalties.** States including New York, California, and Minnesota have increased penalties for wage theft, including treble damages and personal liability for business owners.

**Criminal penalties.** A growing number of states treat intentional wage theft as a criminal offense, not just a civil violation.

**Wage theft prevention notices.** States including New York and California require employers to provide written notice at the time of hire specifying the employee's rate of pay, pay frequency, overtime rate, and employer information.

**Private attorney general acts.** California's Private Attorneys General Act (PAGA) allows employees to file lawsuits on behalf of the state for labor code violations, creating a powerful enforcement mechanism beyond government agencies.

## Compliance Best Practices

**1. Audit your pay practices.** Conduct a thorough review of your payroll practices, including overtime calculations, minimum wage compliance, meal and rest break policies, and tip handling. Compare your practices to the requirements of every jurisdiction where you have employees.

**2. Classify workers correctly.** Misclassification of non-exempt employees as exempt is one of the most common sources of overtime violations. Review every exempt classification against the applicable duties and salary tests.

**3. Implement reliable timekeeping.** Use a timekeeping system that accurately records all hours worked. Prohibit off-the-clock work in writing and enforce the prohibition. Require managers to approve timesheets and investigate discrepancies.

**4. Provide required notices.** Distribute wage theft prevention notices at hire and whenever pay rates change, as required by applicable state law.

**5. Pay final wages on time.** Know the final pay deadline in every state where you have employees and ensure your payroll process can meet it. In some states, the deadline for involuntary termination differs from voluntary resignation.

**6. Train managers.** Supervisors who ask employees to work off the clock, alter timesheets, or skip breaks create liability for the company. Make clear that these practices are prohibited and will result in discipline.

**7. Maintain records.** Keep accurate payroll records for the period required by law, which may be three to four years or longer depending on the jurisdiction. Good records are your best defense against wage theft claims.

Wage theft enforcement is intensifying, and the cost of noncompliance, including back wages, penalties, legal fees, and reputational harm, far exceeds the cost of getting it right the first time.
