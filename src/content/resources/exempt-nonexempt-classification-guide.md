## Why Classification Matters

Misclassifying a non-exempt employee as exempt means that employee may be owed back overtime pay, liquidated damages, and attorney fees under the Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA). When misclassification affects an entire job category, the financial exposure multiplies rapidly. Getting classification right from the start is far less expensive than correcting it later.

## The Two-Part Test

To qualify as exempt from overtime under the FLSA, an employee must generally satisfy both a salary test and a duties test.

### The Salary Test

The employee must be paid on a salary basis at or above the minimum threshold set by the Department of Labor. The salary basis requirement means the employee receives a predetermined, fixed amount each pay period that is not subject to reduction based on variations in the quality or quantity of work performed.

Certain deductions from salary are permitted — for example, full-day absences for personal reasons or unpaid disciplinary suspensions for workplace conduct rule violations imposed under a written policy. However, docking an exempt employee's pay for partial-day absences (other than FMLA leave) can destroy the exemption.

### The Duties Tests

Meeting the salary threshold alone does not make an employee exempt. The employee's actual job duties must fit within one of the recognized exemption categories:

**Executive Exemption**: The employee's primary duty is managing the enterprise or a recognized department, they customarily direct the work of two or more full-time employees, and they have genuine authority to hire, fire, or make recommendations that carry particular weight.

**Administrative Exemption**: The primary duty involves office or non-manual work directly related to management or general business operations, and includes the exercise of discretion and independent judgment on matters of significance.

**Professional Exemption**: The primary duty requires advanced knowledge in a field of science or learning customarily acquired through prolonged specialized intellectual instruction (learned professional), or requires invention, imagination, originality, or talent in a recognized creative field (creative professional).

**Computer Employee Exemption**: Applies to systems analysts, programmers, software engineers, and similar workers whose primary duties involve the application of systems analysis techniques, design or development of computer programs, or a combination of these tasks.

**Outside Sales Exemption**: The employee's primary duty is making sales or obtaining orders away from the employer's place of business. This exemption has no salary requirement.

## Common Classification Errors

**Classifying by job title rather than actual duties.** A "manager" who spends most of the day performing the same tasks as hourly workers may not meet the executive exemption duties test.

**Assuming all salaried employees are exempt.** Paying a salary is necessary for most exemptions but not sufficient. The duties test must also be met.

**Applying the administrative exemption too broadly.** Routine clerical or administrative support work, even if performed in an office, generally does not meet the "discretion and independent judgment" requirement.

**Ignoring state law.** Some states, including California and New York, have stricter exemption standards. California, for instance, requires that exempt employees spend more than 50 percent of their time on exempt duties, and it sets a higher salary threshold.

## Practical Steps for Employers

1. **Audit current classifications.** Review each exempt position against both the salary and duties tests. Focus first on positions where the exemption is least clear.
2. **Base decisions on actual duties.** Observe what people actually do, not what their job description says. Update job descriptions to reflect reality.
3. **Document your analysis.** Record the facts and reasoning supporting each classification decision.
4. **Reclassify promptly when duties change.** Promotions, reorganizations, and shifting responsibilities can change an employee's exempt status.
5. **Train managers on salary basis rules.** Ensure supervisors understand they cannot dock exempt employees' pay for partial-day absences or for quality-of-work issues.

When in doubt about a close call, consult with an employment attorney. The cost of a legal review is a fraction of the cost of defending a misclassification claim.
