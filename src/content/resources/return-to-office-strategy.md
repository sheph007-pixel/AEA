## The Return-to-Office Dilemma

Three years after the pandemic forced a mass remote work experiment, many employers are mandating a return to the office, and many are facing resistance. High-profile return-to-office mandates have been met with employee petitions, increased turnover, and, in some cases, union organizing activity. At the same time, employers have legitimate reasons for wanting teams together, including collaboration, culture, mentoring, and management concerns.

The question is not whether to bring people back but how to do it in a way that serves your business goals without driving away the talent you spent years developing.

## Define Your Business Case

Before announcing any return mandate, clearly articulate why in-office work is necessary. Vague rationales like "collaboration" or "culture" without specifics will not persuade employees who have been performing well remotely.

Strong business cases for in-person work include:

- Roles that require hands-on work with equipment, materials, or clients
- New employee onboarding and training that benefits from in-person interaction
- Specific collaborative projects that are demonstrably more effective in person
- Client-facing roles where physical presence is expected

If you cannot articulate a clear business reason for a specific role to be in-office, consider whether the mandate is necessary for that role.

## Design a Hybrid Model

Most employers are finding that a hybrid model, combining some in-office days with remote flexibility, achieves better results than a full return mandate. When designing your hybrid approach:

**Set clear expectations.** Specify which days or how many days per week employees are expected in the office. Ambiguity creates frustration and inconsistent enforcement.

**Anchor in-office days to purpose.** Rather than arbitrary days, schedule in-office time around activities that benefit from being together: team meetings, project kickoffs, one-on-ones, training sessions, and collaborative work sessions.

**Maintain remote infrastructure.** Continue investing in the tools and processes that make remote work effective. Hybrid models fail when they are designed entirely around the in-office experience, leaving remote participants as second-class participants.

**Accommodate individual circumstances.** Some employees relocated during the pandemic, some have caregiving responsibilities that make rigid schedules difficult, and some have disabilities that remote work accommodates. Build in a process for requesting exceptions or modifications.

## Communication and Timing

**Give adequate notice.** A reasonable transition period is essential. Employees who relocated, arranged childcare, or structured their lives around remote work need time to adjust. Ninety days is a common minimum for significant schedule changes.

**Explain the reasons.** Share the business case openly. Employees are more likely to accept changes they understand, even if they do not prefer them.

**Listen to concerns.** Create channels for employees to raise concerns and ask questions. Town halls, surveys, and one-on-one conversations help surface issues before they become attrition events.

**Acknowledge what was gained.** Recognize that remote work worked for many employees and that the flexibility it provided was valued. A mandate that dismisses the remote experience as an aberration rather than an achievement alienates employees.

## Legal Considerations

Return-to-office mandates can trigger legal issues in several areas:

- **ADA accommodations**: Employees with disabilities who were effectively accommodated by remote work may be entitled to continued remote work as a reasonable accommodation.
- **FMLA and state leave laws**: Employees on protected leave cannot be penalized for absence from the office.
- **Disparate impact**: If a return mandate disproportionately affects employees of a particular protected class (for example, women who bear a greater share of childcare), it could face legal scrutiny.
- **NLRA implications**: Employee collective action to resist return mandates may be protected concerted activity under the NLRA.

Consult employment counsel before implementing a mandate, particularly if you anticipate significant employee resistance.

## Measuring Success

After implementation, track metrics that indicate whether the return is achieving its goals: collaboration output, employee engagement scores, turnover rates, recruitment success, and productivity measures. If turnover spikes significantly, revisit your approach rather than attributing departures solely to employees who "did not want to work."

The employers managing this transition most successfully are those who treat it as a negotiation, not an edict. They explain their reasoning, offer genuine flexibility, and adjust based on results.
