A job posting is your first communication with potential candidates, and it significantly influences who applies. An effective posting attracts qualified, diverse candidates while accurately representing the role and your organization. A poorly written posting wastes recruiting resources and may expose you to legal risk.

## Structure of an Effective Job Posting

Organize your posting for maximum impact and readability:

- **Compelling headline**: Use a clear, accurate job title. Avoid internal titles or jargon that candidates will not search for.
- **Opening hook**: Lead with one or two sentences about why this role matters and what makes your organization a great place to work
- **Key responsibilities**: List the five to eight most important duties using action verbs
- **Requirements**: Separate must-have qualifications from nice-to-have qualifications
- **Compensation and benefits**: Include salary range and key benefits
- **About the company**: Brief description of your organization, culture, and mission
- **How to apply**: Clear instructions on the application process

## Legal Compliance in Job Postings

Several legal requirements affect what you can and should include:

- **Pay transparency laws**: A growing number of states and cities require salary ranges in job postings, including Colorado, California, New York, and Washington
- **EEO statement**: Include your equal employment opportunity commitment
- **Non-discriminatory language**: Avoid terms that could discourage protected groups from applying
- **Accurate requirements**: Only include qualifications that are genuinely necessary for the job to avoid disparate impact claims
- **OFCCP requirements**: Federal contractors must include specific language about affirmative action and EEO

## Writing Inclusive Job Postings

Research shows that the language in job postings significantly affects who applies:

- Use gender-neutral language throughout (avoid "he/she," use "they" or "you")
- Limit requirements to what is truly necessary; studies show that women and minorities are more likely to self-select out when they do not meet every listed qualification
- Replace "requirements" with "what we're looking for" to reduce the perception that every item is mandatory
- Avoid jargon, acronyms, and internal terminology
- Emphasize learning and growth opportunities
- Include a statement encouraging candidates who may not meet every qualification to apply

## Optimizing for Visibility

Ensure your posting reaches the widest possible audience:

- Use standard job titles that candidates actually search for
- Include relevant keywords naturally throughout the posting
- Keep the posting concise (500-800 words is generally optimal)
- Format with bullet points and short paragraphs for easy scanning
- Post on multiple platforms including general job boards, industry-specific sites, and professional associations
- Share on social media and through employee referral networks

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

These errors undermine your recruiting efforts:

- Listing an unrealistic number of requirements that no single candidate can meet
- Using superlatives like "rock star" or "ninja" that can signal exclusivity
- Burying the salary range or omitting it entirely where not required by law
- Including discriminatory language such as "young and energetic" or "must be a native English speaker" when not a bona fide requirement
- Posting a job description rather than a job posting (descriptions are internal documents; postings are marketing materials)
- Failing to update postings when the role changes
