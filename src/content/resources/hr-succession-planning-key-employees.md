## The Importance of Succession Planning

Succession planning identifies and develops internal candidates to fill critical leadership and specialized positions when incumbents leave. Research consistently shows that organizations with formal succession plans experience smoother leadership transitions, better employee retention, and stronger financial performance than those without.

Despite its importance, many organizations lack formal succession plans. A sudden departure of a key leader or specialist without a prepared successor can disrupt operations, damage client relationships, erode institutional knowledge, and create competitive vulnerability.

## Identifying Critical Positions

The first step in succession planning is identifying positions whose vacancy would significantly impact business operations. These include C-suite and senior leadership roles, positions requiring specialized technical knowledge, client-facing roles with deep relationship portfolios, and any role where the incumbent possesses institutional knowledge that would be difficult to replace.

For each critical position, assess the current incumbent's expected tenure and retirement timeline, the complexity of the role, the availability of internal candidates, and the time required to develop a successor. This assessment helps prioritize succession planning efforts and allocate development resources effectively.

## Developing Succession Candidates

Once critical positions and potential successors are identified, the organization should create individual development plans for each candidate. Development activities may include stretch assignments that expose candidates to new responsibilities, cross-functional projects that broaden business knowledge, mentoring relationships with current leaders, formal education and professional development programs, and temporary assignments or job rotations.

The development timeline should align with the anticipated succession timeline. A successor who will be needed within 12 months requires an accelerated development path, while a longer-term candidate can benefit from a more gradual progression.

## Knowledge Transfer and Documentation

Effective succession planning includes deliberate knowledge transfer from current incumbents to potential successors. This involves documenting critical processes, relationships, and institutional knowledge that resides primarily with the incumbent. Job shadowing, co-management periods, and structured knowledge transfer meetings help capture and transmit tacit knowledge.

Creating written process documentation, relationship maps, and decision frameworks ensures that critical knowledge is preserved even if the knowledge transfer period is shortened by an unexpected departure.

## Monitoring and Updating the Plan

Succession plans should be reviewed at least annually and updated when organizational changes, personnel changes, or strategic shifts occur. The review should assess the readiness of identified successors, update development plans, identify new critical positions, and address any gaps in the succession pipeline. Treating succession planning as an ongoing process rather than a one-time exercise ensures the organization maintains a viable leadership pipeline.
