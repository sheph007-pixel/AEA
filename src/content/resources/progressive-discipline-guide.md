Progressive discipline is a structured approach to correcting employee behavior and performance issues through escalating steps. While at-will employment allows termination for any legal reason, a progressive discipline policy demonstrates fairness, provides documentation, and reduces the risk of wrongful termination claims.

## Typical Progressive Discipline Steps

Most progressive discipline policies include four stages:

- **Verbal warning**: A documented conversation about the issue, expectations, and consequences of continued behavior
- **Written warning**: A formal written notice describing the issue, prior conversations, expected improvement, and a timeline
- **Final written warning or suspension**: A serious step indicating that termination will follow if improvement does not occur
- **Termination**: Ending the employment relationship after prior steps have failed

Each step should include clear documentation of the problem, the expected behavior, a reasonable timeline for improvement, and the consequences of failure to improve.

## When to Skip Steps

Not every situation requires starting at step one. Certain conduct warrants immediate escalation or termination:

- Theft or fraud
- Workplace violence or threats
- Possession of weapons on company property
- Being under the influence of drugs or alcohol at work
- Gross insubordination
- Serious safety violations that endanger others
- Sexual harassment or other egregious misconduct

Your policy should clearly state that the company reserves the right to skip steps or proceed directly to termination depending on the severity of the conduct.

## Documentation Best Practices

Documentation is the backbone of effective progressive discipline:

- Record the date, time, and location of each conversation
- Describe the specific behavior or performance issue with factual details
- Reference the policy or expectation that was violated
- Include the employee's explanation or response
- State the expected improvement and timeline
- Outline the consequences of continued problems
- Have the employee sign acknowledging receipt (not agreement)
- Keep copies in the employee's personnel file

If an employee refuses to sign, note the refusal on the document and have a witness sign confirming the document was presented.

## Consistency Is Critical

Inconsistent application of discipline is one of the most common reasons employers lose employment lawsuits. To maintain consistency:

- Train all managers on the policy and their role in applying it
- Have HR review proposed disciplinary actions before they are delivered
- Track discipline across departments to identify patterns or disparities
- Apply the same standards regardless of the employee's protected characteristics, tenure, or personal relationship with management
- Document legitimate business reasons for any deviation from standard practice

## Preserving At-Will Employment

A common concern is that progressive discipline policies create an implied contract that undermines at-will employment. To mitigate this risk:

- Include a clear at-will disclaimer in the policy and employee handbook
- State that the policy is a guideline, not a binding contract
- Specify that management retains discretion to determine the appropriate level of discipline
- Avoid language that could be interpreted as a promise of continued employment
- Have employees sign an acknowledgment of the at-will relationship
