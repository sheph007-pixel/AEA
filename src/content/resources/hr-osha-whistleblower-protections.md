## Scope of OSHA Whistleblower Protections

OSHA administers whistleblower protections under more than 20 federal statutes, extending far beyond workplace safety complaints. In addition to Section 11(c) of the Occupational Safety and Health Act, OSHA enforces anti-retaliation provisions under the Sarbanes-Oxley Act, the Affordable Care Act, the Consumer Financial Protection Act, various environmental laws, transportation safety laws, and nuclear safety laws.

These statutes protect employees who report violations, refuse to participate in illegal activity, or cooperate with government investigations. The breadth of OSHA's whistleblower jurisdiction means that employers in virtually every industry may face whistleblower complaints.

## What Constitutes Protected Activity

Protected activity under these statutes generally includes filing complaints with government agencies about workplace safety hazards or legal violations, reporting concerns internally to supervisors or compliance departments, refusing to perform work that the employee reasonably believes poses an imminent danger, participating in government investigations or proceedings, and testifying in hearings related to protected matters.

The employee need not be correct about the underlying violation to be protected. The standard in most cases is whether the employee had a reasonable, good-faith belief that a violation occurred. This means employers cannot retaliate even if the employee's complaint is ultimately determined to be unfounded.

## Prohibited Employer Actions

Retaliation includes any adverse action taken because of the employee's protected activity. Adverse actions include termination, demotion, suspension, reduction in hours or pay, reassignment to less desirable work, blacklisting, threats, intimidation, and any other action that would dissuade a reasonable employee from engaging in protected activity.

The timing between the protected activity and the adverse action is often the most significant evidence in whistleblower cases. Actions taken shortly after an employee engages in protected activity invite scrutiny and create a strong inference of retaliation.

## Responding to Whistleblower Complaints

When OSHA receives a whistleblower complaint, it investigates to determine whether a violation occurred. Employers typically receive notice of the complaint and an opportunity to respond. The employer should preserve all documents related to the complainant's employment, including performance evaluations, disciplinary records, and communications. Demonstrating that the adverse action was based on legitimate, non-retaliatory reasons supported by documentation is the primary defense.

Employers should respond promptly and thoroughly to OSHA investigations. Failure to cooperate can result in adverse findings. If OSHA finds merit in the complaint, it may order reinstatement, back pay, compensatory damages, and attorney fees.

## Prevention Strategies

Employers should train managers on whistleblower protections and the prohibition against retaliation, establish clear channels for employees to report concerns internally before going to government agencies, document all employment decisions with legitimate business reasons, implement policies that protect employees who raise safety or compliance concerns, and separate the handling of employee complaints from personnel decisions about the complaining employee.
