## A Fractured Legal Landscape

As more states legalize recreational and medical cannabis, employer drug testing policies face increasing legal complexity. The traditional approach of testing for marijuana and treating a positive result as grounds for adverse action is no longer viable in many jurisdictions. Yet cannabis remains a Schedule I substance under federal law, and federal contractors, safety-sensitive industries, and DOT-regulated employers still have testing obligations.

Employers must navigate a patchwork of state laws that vary dramatically in the protections they afford to cannabis users.

## Categories of State Protections

State laws fall into several broad categories:

**No employment protections.** Some states have legalized cannabis but provide no protections for employees. Employers in these states can generally continue to test for marijuana and make employment decisions based on positive results.

**Medical cannabis protections.** Many states prohibit employers from discriminating against employees or applicants who are registered medical cannabis users, with exceptions for safety-sensitive positions and situations where accommodation would violate federal law. States in this category include Arizona, Connecticut, Delaware, Illinois, Minnesota, New York, and Pennsylvania, among others.

**Off-duty use protections.** A growing number of states prohibit employers from taking adverse action based on off-duty cannabis use. California's AB 2188 (effective January 2024) prohibits employment discrimination based on off-duty marijuana use and bars employers from using hair or urine tests that detect non-psychoactive cannabis metabolites, as these indicate past use rather than current impairment. Washington and other states have similar protections.

**Pre-employment testing restrictions.** Some jurisdictions, including New York City and Philadelphia, restrict or prohibit pre-employment marijuana testing for most positions.

## Practical Steps for Employers

**1. Review your testing policy state by state.** A single national drug testing policy may no longer be compliant. Identify every jurisdiction where you have employees and review the applicable cannabis employment protections in each one.

**2. Distinguish between impairment and use.** The central challenge with cannabis testing is that standard urine tests detect metabolites that can remain in the body for weeks after use, long after any impairing effects have subsided. Where laws protect off-duty use, relying on these tests for employment decisions is legally risky.

**3. Consider impairment-based approaches.** Rather than testing for the presence of a substance, focus on observable signs of impairment at work. Train supervisors to document specific, objective observations: slurred speech, unsteady gait, the smell of marijuana, impaired coordination, or erratic behavior. Reasonable suspicion testing based on documented observations remains defensible in most jurisdictions.

**4. Preserve safety-sensitive testing.** Most state cannabis protections include exceptions for safety-sensitive positions. Identify positions where impairment creates a genuine safety risk, such as operating heavy machinery, driving, or working at heights, and document why testing is necessary for those roles.

**5. Know your federal obligations.** Employers subject to federal drug testing requirements, including DOT-regulated industries, federal contractors, and employers in industries governed by the Drug-Free Workplace Act, must continue to comply with federal law regardless of state cannabis legalization. Marijuana remains a tested substance under the DOT testing protocol.

**6. Update your employee handbook.** Revise your drug policy to reflect current law. Clearly state what substances are prohibited, when testing will occur, what constitutes reasonable suspicion, and the consequences of policy violations. Include language addressing the intersection of state cannabis laws and your specific obligations.

**7. Train supervisors.** Equip managers with the knowledge to make reasonable suspicion determinations based on observable impairment rather than assumptions. Provide clear documentation forms and a defined process for escalation.

## The Impairment Testing Gap

One practical challenge employers face is the lack of a reliable real-time impairment test for cannabis equivalent to a breathalyzer for alcohol. Several companies are developing oral fluid tests and cognitive impairment assessments, but no single tool has achieved widespread adoption or legal validation. For now, reasonable suspicion based on trained observation remains the primary tool.

## Looking Ahead

The trend toward employee protections for cannabis use will continue. Employers who proactively shift from substance detection to impairment management will be better positioned for compliance as laws evolve. Review your policies annually and consult employment counsel in every jurisdiction where you operate.
