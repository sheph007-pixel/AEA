Federal COVID-19 relief legislation created several tax credits and deferrals designed to help employers retain workers and manage cash flow. Understanding and properly claiming these credits can significantly reduce your tax burden. Here is a practical overview of the major employer-focused provisions.

## Employee Retention Credit (ERC)

The Employee Retention Credit, introduced by the CARES Act and subsequently expanded, is a refundable payroll tax credit for eligible employers.

### 2020 ERC provisions
- **Eligible employers:** Those who experienced either a full or partial suspension of operations due to government orders or a significant decline in gross receipts (50% decline compared to the same quarter in 2019)
- **Credit amount:** 50% of qualified wages, up to $10,000 in wages per employee for the entire year (maximum credit of $5,000 per employee for 2020)
- **Qualified wages:** For employers with more than 100 full-time employees, only wages paid to employees who were not providing services qualify. For employers with 100 or fewer full-time employees, all employee wages qualify whether or not the employees were providing services.
- **Health plan expenses:** Employer-paid health insurance costs allocable to qualified wages are included in the credit calculation

### Important limitations for 2020
- Employers who received a PPP loan were originally ineligible for the ERC, but the Consolidated Appropriations Act of 2021 retroactively changed this rule to allow employers to claim both, provided they do not use the same wages for both programs
- The credit is claimed against the employer's share of Social Security taxes
- Excess credits are refundable

### How to claim
- File Form 941-X (Adjusted Employer's Quarterly Federal Tax Return) for each applicable quarter
- Reduce wage deductions on the income tax return by the amount of the ERC claimed
- Retain documentation of eligibility, including evidence of government orders and gross receipts calculations

## FFCRA Paid Leave Tax Credits

Employers who provided paid leave under the Families First Coronavirus Response Act receive dollar-for-dollar tax credits:

- Credits cover 100% of qualified leave wages paid, up to the applicable daily and aggregate caps
- Credits also cover the employer's share of Medicare tax on leave wages and the cost of maintaining health insurance during the leave period
- Credits are taken against the employer's share of Social Security taxes and are refundable
- For the mandatory period (April 1 through December 31, 2020), all covered employers must provide the leave and may claim the credit
- Subsequent legislation extended voluntary leave with credits into 2021

## Payroll Tax Deferral

The CARES Act allowed all employers to defer the deposit of the employer's share of Social Security taxes:

- **Applicable period:** March 27, 2020 through December 31, 2020
- **Repayment schedule:** 50% due by December 31, 2021; remaining 50% due by December 31, 2022
- **No interest or penalties** accrue during the deferral period if payments are made on time
- The deferral applies to the 6.2% employer share of Social Security taxes only, not the Medicare tax or the employee's share

This deferral was available to all employers regardless of size, and it could be combined with PPP loans and the ERC.

## Work Opportunity Tax Credit (WOTC)

While not new to the pandemic, the WOTC provides credits for hiring individuals from targeted groups who face barriers to employment:

- Credits range from $2,400 to $9,600 per qualified new hire depending on the target group and hours worked
- Target groups include veterans, SNAP recipients, ex-felons, and long-term unemployment recipients, among others
- Employers must obtain certification from their state workforce agency (Form 8850 must be submitted within 28 days of the employee's start date)

As unemployment rose during the pandemic, more job applicants fell into WOTC-eligible categories, making this credit more broadly available.

## Coordination Between Credits

Employers must be careful not to double-count wages:

- Wages used for PPP forgiveness cannot be used for the ERC
- Wages used for the ERC cannot also be used for the WOTC
- FFCRA leave wages used for the FFCRA credit cannot also be used for the ERC
- Proper allocation requires detailed payroll records and careful tracking

## Practical Steps

1. **Assess eligibility** for each credit based on your specific circumstances
2. **Segregate payroll records** to track which wages are allocated to which program
3. **Work with a qualified tax advisor** who understands the interaction between these credits
4. **File amended returns** if you discover retroactive eligibility (particularly relevant for the ERC after the PPP restriction was lifted)
5. **Maintain thorough documentation** including government orders, gross receipts records, and payroll detail for at least seven years

These credits represent substantial financial relief for eligible employers. However, the complexity of the rules and the interaction between programs make professional guidance a worthwhile investment.
