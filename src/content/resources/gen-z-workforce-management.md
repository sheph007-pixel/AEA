## A New Generation of Workers

Generation Z, generally defined as those born between 1997 and 2012, now makes up a growing share of the workforce. As these workers move from entry-level positions into roles with greater responsibility, employers need to understand their expectations and adapt management practices accordingly, without alienating other generations in the process.

This is not about stereotyping or catering to one demographic. It is about recognizing that workforce expectations evolve and that effective employers adjust.

## What the Research Shows

Multiple workforce studies consistently identify several themes among Gen Z employees:

**Transparency about pay and advancement.** Gen Z workers entered the job market alongside pay transparency laws and widespread online salary data. They expect to know what a position pays, how raises are determined, and what the path to promotion looks like. Opaque compensation practices that older workers may have accepted are a significant source of frustration and turnover for younger employees.

**Purpose and values alignment.** Gen Z workers are more likely to evaluate employers based on stated values, environmental practices, and social impact. This does not mean they expect their employer to be an activist organization, but they do notice when stated values and actual practices diverge.

**Mental health support.** This generation is more open about mental health and more likely to seek support. They expect employers to offer mental health resources, normalize their use, and create an environment where seeking help is not stigmatized.

**Feedback frequency.** Annual performance reviews feel inadequate to workers who grew up with constant feedback loops. Gen Z employees generally prefer regular check-ins, immediate feedback on projects, and ongoing conversations about development.

**Flexibility.** Having entered the workforce during or after the pandemic, many Gen Z workers view remote and hybrid work as normal, not a perk. Rigid in-office requirements without clear business justification are a common reason for declining offers or leaving positions.

**Technology as baseline.** Gen Z expects modern, functional technology tools. Outdated systems, paper-based processes, and clunky software frustrate workers who have been digitally native their entire lives.

## Practical Management Adjustments

### Revamp Onboarding

First impressions matter disproportionately for younger workers who have many options. Provide a structured onboarding experience that includes clear job expectations, introduction to team members and culture, early feedback opportunities, and a designated mentor or buddy.

### Increase Feedback Cadence

Supplement annual reviews with monthly or biweekly one-on-one meetings focused on development, not just task management. These do not need to be long or formal. A fifteen-minute conversation about what is going well, what could improve, and what support is needed goes a long way.

### Be Transparent About Paths

Document career paths and share them openly. If advancement requires specific skills, certifications, or tenure, say so. If a role is a dead end without lateral movement, be honest about that too. Ambiguity about advancement is a top driver of early-career turnover.

### Invest in Development

Provide learning opportunities including mentoring, cross-functional projects, skills training, and conference attendance. Gen Z workers who feel they are not growing will seek growth elsewhere. The investment in development pays for itself through reduced turnover and a more capable workforce.

### Leverage Their Strengths

Gen Z workers often bring strong digital literacy, comfort with diverse teams and perspectives, and an ability to adapt quickly to new tools and platforms. Channel these strengths by involving them in technology selection, social media strategy, and process improvement initiatives.

## Avoiding the Generational Trap

The worst approach to generational differences is treating them as a zero-sum game. Policies that benefit Gen Z workers, such as transparent pay practices, flexible scheduling, regular feedback, and mental health support, are generally valued by employees of all ages. Frame changes as improvements to the overall employee experience, not accommodations for one group.

Effective management has always meant understanding what your people need and adjusting accordingly. Gen Z is not an alien species. They are workers with expectations shaped by their environment, just like every generation before them.
