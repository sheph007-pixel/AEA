## Who Must File

The EEO-1 Component 1 report is an annual filing required by the EEOC for private employers with 100 or more employees, and for federal contractors with 50 or more employees and a contract of $50,000 or more. Each establishment (physical location) with 50 or more employees requires a separate report, along with a consolidated report for the company.

Failing to file can result in compulsory process from the EEOC and potential legal consequences. More practically, the filing is straightforward if you prepare properly.

## What Is Reported

The EEO-1 report collects workforce demographic data by job category and by race/ethnicity and sex. Employers report the number of employees in each combination of:

**Job categories** (10 categories): Executive/senior level officials and managers, first/mid-level officials and managers, professionals, technicians, sales workers, administrative support workers, craft workers, operatives, laborers and helpers, and service workers.

**Race/ethnicity categories** (7 categories): Hispanic or Latino, White, Black or African American, Native Hawaiian or Other Pacific Islander, Asian, American Indian or Alaska Native, and Two or More Races.

**Sex** (2 categories): Male and Female.

The data is reported as a snapshot of your workforce on a single chosen date, typically a pay period between October and December of the reporting year.

## Step-by-Step Process

### 1. Determine Your Filing Obligation

Confirm whether your organization meets the filing threshold. Count all employees, including part-time and temporary workers, to determine if you reach 100 employees (or 50 for federal contractors).

### 2. Select Your Workforce Snapshot Date

Choose a pay period in Q4 of the reporting year. Use a payroll report from that period as your data source. The snapshot should represent your typical workforce, not a seasonal peak or valley if possible.

### 3. Classify Employees by Job Category

Map each employee to one of the ten EEO-1 job categories based on their actual duties, not their job title. This is often the most time-consuming step because job titles rarely align neatly with EEO-1 categories. Focus on the primary duties performed.

### 4. Compile Demographic Data

Pull race/ethnicity and sex data from your HRIS. This data is typically collected through voluntary self-identification during the hiring process. If an employee has not self-identified, the employer may use observer identification or employment records, but self-identification is preferred.

### 5. Prepare Multi-Establishment Reports

If your company has multiple locations, determine which require separate reports. Establishments with 50 or more employees always require individual reports. Smaller establishments can be aggregated, but certain rules apply to the aggregation.

### 6. File Through the EEO-1 Online Portal

The EEOC provides an online filing system. Create or verify your account well before the deadline. Upload your data in the required format, review it for accuracy, and submit. You will receive a confirmation.

## Common Errors and How to Avoid Them

**Incorrect job category assignments.** Review the EEOC's job category descriptions carefully. A common mistake is placing all salaried employees in the "professionals" category when many belong in administrative support, technicians, or other categories based on their actual duties.

**Missing establishments.** Employers with multiple locations sometimes fail to file for all establishments. Maintain a current list of all locations and their headcounts.

**Using the wrong snapshot period.** The data must reflect a specific pay period, not a monthly average or year-end count.

**Incomplete demographic data.** While voluntary, gaps in self-identification data weaken the accuracy of your report. Encourage self-identification during onboarding and periodically thereafter, emphasizing that the information is used for federal reporting and will not be used in employment decisions.

## Using the Data

Beyond compliance, EEO-1 data provides a valuable internal tool. Analyzing your workforce composition by job category can reveal patterns in representation, highlight areas where recruiting and promotion practices may need attention, and provide a baseline for measuring the effectiveness of your talent strategies.

Retain copies of your filed reports and supporting data for at least three years, though longer retention is advisable given the statute of limitations for discrimination claims.
