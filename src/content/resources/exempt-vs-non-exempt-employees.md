Misclassifying employees as exempt or non-exempt is one of the most common and costly compliance errors employers make. Under the Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA), non-exempt employees must receive overtime pay at one and a half times their regular rate for hours worked beyond 40 in a workweek. Exempt employees are not entitled to overtime.

## The Three-Part Exemption Test

To qualify as exempt under the FLSA, an employee must meet all three criteria:

- **Salary basis test**: The employee must be paid a predetermined, fixed salary that is not subject to reduction based on quality or quantity of work
- **Salary level test**: The employee must earn at least the minimum salary threshold set by the Department of Labor
- **Duties test**: The employee's primary job duties must fall within one of the recognized exemption categories

Failing any one of these tests means the employee is non-exempt and entitled to overtime.

## Common Exemption Categories

The FLSA recognizes several "white collar" exemptions:

- **Executive exemption**: Manages the enterprise or a recognized department, directs the work of two or more employees, and has authority to hire or fire
- **Administrative exemption**: Performs office or non-manual work directly related to management or business operations and exercises independent judgment on significant matters
- **Professional exemption**: Performs work requiring advanced knowledge in a field of science or learning, or creative work requiring invention or talent
- **Computer employee exemption**: Works as a computer systems analyst, programmer, software engineer, or similar role
- **Outside sales exemption**: Regularly makes sales or obtains contracts away from the employer's place of business

## Common Classification Mistakes

Employers frequently make these errors:

- Classifying someone as exempt based on job title alone rather than actual duties
- Assuming salaried employees are automatically exempt
- Failing to account for state laws that may impose stricter requirements
- Not reassessing classification when job duties change
- Docking an exempt employee's pay for partial-day absences, which can destroy the salary basis

## Consequences of Misclassification

Getting classification wrong can be expensive:

- Back pay for unpaid overtime, potentially going back two years (three years for willful violations)
- Liquidated damages equal to the back pay amount
- Attorney fees and court costs
- State penalties, which vary by jurisdiction
- Department of Labor investigations that may expand beyond the initial complaint

## Best Practices for Employers

To reduce misclassification risk:

- Review actual job duties, not just job descriptions
- Audit classifications periodically, especially after reorganizations
- Consult the Department of Labor's Fact Sheets for guidance on specific exemptions
- Check state law requirements, which may differ from federal standards
- Document the analysis supporting each classification decision
- Train managers not to assign duties inconsistent with an employee's exempt status
